# Three point summary

-   A parabolic PDE describes how a function evolves over time under the
    influence of an elliptic operator. Unlike their elliptic
    counterparts, parabolic PDEs are typically
    [well-posed](https://nowheredifferentiable.com/2024-02-28-PDEs-6-Elliptic_PDE._Well_posedness_and_regularity/#:~:text=is%20well%2Dposed.-,Definition%202,-(Well%2Dposedness)).
    For well-behaved coefficients, a unique solution always exists and
    depends continuously on the initial data.

-   Solutions to parabolic PDEs can be viewed as functions mapping an
    instant in time to a function in a Banach space. From this
    viewpoint, the PDE becomes an infinite dimensional ODE. The Galerkin
    method is a powerful tool to prove the well-posedness of the problem
    and approximate its solutions using a finite-dimensional ODE that
    approximates the original infinite-dimensional problem.

-   The solution is smoother than the initial data and will always be at
    least continuous in time and (weakly) differentiable in space. If
    the coefficients of the PDE are smooth, the solution will be smooth
    as well.

# Notation

-   As in the rest of the series, we will let $U$ denote an arbitrary
    open subset of $\mathbb{R}^d$. That is, it may be bounded or
    unbounded with no conditions on the regularity of the boundary (if
    it exists). To denote a smooth domain, we will use the notation
    $\Omega$.

-   We will be working with functions of time and space $u(t,x)$ defined
    on some time interval $I=[0, T]$ and spatial domain $U$. We will
    denote by $u(t)$ the function $u(t,\cdot):U\to \mathbb{R}$.

-   Given a topological vector space $X$ with dual $X'$ and
    $x \in X, w \in X'$ we write the *duality pairing* $$\begin{aligned}
     (x,w):= w(x).
         
    \end{aligned}$$

# Introduction

In the [previous post of this
series](https://nowheredifferentiable.com/2024-02-28-PDEs-6-Elliptic_PDE._Well_posedness_and_regularity/),
we studied the well-posedness and regularity of solutions to an elliptic
PDE of the form $$\begin{aligned}
  \begin{cases}
    \mathcal{L}u = f & \text{in } U     \\
 u = 0   & \text{on } \partial U
  \end{cases}
\end{aligned}$$ where the second order differential operator
$\mathcal{L}$ was given in divergence form as $$\begin{aligned}
\label{elliptic operator 0}
 -\nabla \cdot (\bm{A} \nabla u)+ \nabla \cdot (\bm{b} u)+cu.
\end{aligned}$$ and verified the ellipticity condition. After defining
the weak formulation of the problem, we naturally obtained the function
spaces we were interested in and, under some restrictions on the
coefficients, proved the existence and uniqueness of solutions. When
solutions were not guaranteed to be unique, we studied the spectrum of
$\mathcal{L}$, and in all cases, we showed that the solution had
improved regularity as compared to the coefficients of the PDE.

In this post we now look to mimic the previous analysis but for
parabolic PDEs. We define the operator $$\begin{aligned}
\label{elliptic operator}
 \mathcal{L}u:= -\nabla \cdot (\bm{A} \nabla u)+ \bm{b} \cdot \nabla u+cu,
\end{aligned}$$ (by Leibnit'z rule one can move between
[\[elliptic operator 0\]](#elliptic operator 0){reference-type="eqref"
reference="elliptic operator 0"} and
[\[elliptic operator\]](#elliptic operator){reference-type="eqref"
reference="elliptic operator"}) and consider the parabolic PDE
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{parabolic}
  \begin{cases}
    \partial_t u + \mathcal{L}u = f & \text{in } I\times U      \\
 u(0) = g         & \text{on } U          \\
 u = 0          & \text{on } I\times \partial U,
  \end{cases}
\end{aligned}$$ where $I=[0,T]$ is the time interval of interest and
$0<T< \infty$. Here, we need an extra initial condition $u(0) = g$ that
tells us the initial state of the system. The operator $\mathcal{L}$ has
the same form as in
[\[elliptic operator\]](#elliptic operator){reference-type="eqref"
reference="elliptic operator"}, however now the coefficients
$\bm{A}(t, \bm{x}), \bm{b}(t, \bm{x}), c(t, \bm{x})$ are allowed to
depend on both time and space. In the next sections, we will define the
weak formulation of the problem and study the well-posedness and
regularity of solutions. As we will see, within the functions spaces of
interest, [\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"} is always well-posed. This is in contrast to
elliptic PDEs, where well-posedness was not guaranteed in general.

# Weak formulation

## Banach valued functions

To define weak solutions to
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"}, it is convenient to switch our viewpoint.
Instead of thinking of $u$ as a real-valued function of time and space,
we think of it as a *Banach space valued function of time*
$$\begin{aligned}
 u: I \to X, \quad t \mapsto u(t),
\end{aligned}$$ where $X$ is some Banach space of function on $U$ (such
as $L^2(U), H_0^1(U),...$) and we use the notation $u(t)(x):= u(t,x)$.
This way of viewing $u$ is an essential tool in the theory of evolution
PDEs, which transform the PDE
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"} into an *infinite dimensional linear ODE*.

To be able to proceed, we need to define an integral on functions valued
in Banach spaces. This integral is called the [Bochner
integral](https://nowheredifferentiable.com/2022-05-27-The-Bochner-integral/).
To briefly summarize, given a separable Banach space $X$ and
$p \in [1, \infty]$ we define $$\begin{aligned}
\label{dual}
 L^p(I \to X)= \left\{f: I \to X: f \text{ measurable and } \left\lVert f \right\rVert_{L^p(I \to X)}< \infty\right\},
\end{aligned}$$ where functions equal almost everywhere are identified,
and the norm is defined as $$\begin{aligned}
\left\lVert f \right\rVert_{L^p(I,X)}  &:= \left( \int_I \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert_X^p \,\mathrm{d}t \right)^{1/p}, \quad p \in [1, \infty),\\
  \|f\|_{L^{\infty}(I\rightarrow X)}&:=\inf \{r>0: \mu(\|f\|>r)=0\}.
\end{aligned}$$ Then, hte spaces $L^p(I \to X)$ are Banach spaces and
functions $f$ in $L^1(I \to X)$ have a well defined Bochner integral
$$\begin{aligned}
  \int_I f(t) \,\mathrm{d}t \in X.
\end{aligned}$$ This integral generalizes the Lebesgue integral and is
also constructed by approximating the integrable functions by simple
functions of the form $\sum_{i=1}^n 1_{A_i} x_i$ where $A_i$ are sets
with finite measure and $x_i \in X$.

Many familiar properties carry over to the Bochner integral. Namely,
integration is a continuous linear operator on $L^1(I \to X)$.
Furthermore, given $p \in [1,\infty)$ and $v \in L^{p'}(I \to X')$,
where $p'$ is the conjugate exponent of $p$, we can define the duality
pairing $$\begin{aligned}
 (u,v):= \int_I (u(t),v(t))_X \,\mathrm{d}t, \quad u \in L^p(I \to X).
\end{aligned}$$ With this identification, the dual of $L^p(I \to X)$ is
equal to $L^{p'}(I \to X')$ . We will also use that if
$\phi_n \in C^\infty(I)$ is a [smooth approximation of
unity](https://nowheredifferentiable.com/2023-07-12-PDEs-3-Sobolev_spaces/#smooth%20section:~:text=is%20an-,approximation,-to%20unity%20if)
then, for $p \in [1, \infty)$ $$\begin{aligned}
\label{approximation}
 \lim_{n \to \infty} u * \phi_n \to u \text{ in } L^p(I \to X),\quad \quad \lim_{n \to \infty} u * \phi_n=u \text{ almost everywhere }. 
\end{aligned}$$

To make the notation more readable we will use the convention of
denoting function spaces in temporal and spatial domains by the
subindexes $t,x$. For example, we write $$\begin{aligned}
 L^2_t H^1_{0,x} & := L^2(I \to H^1_0(U)), \quad L^2_t H^{-1}_{x} := L^2(I \to H^{-1}(U))   \\
 L_{t,x}^\infty & := L^\infty(I \times U), \quad\quad\qquad\quad  L^2_x     := L^2(U).
\end{aligned}$$ Due to the previous discussion we have that
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{duality}
 L^2_t H^1_{0,x}(U)' = L^2_t H^{-1}_{x}(U).
\end{aligned}$$

A tricky aspect of PDE is figuring out what space $X$ should be? The
choice of $X$ needs to be guided by what bounds one can obtain in the
norm of $X$. As we will see when we derive energy bounds for $u$, the
space $X=H_0^1(U)$ is the natural Banach space to consider in this
setting.

## Weak solutions

As may have become familiar at this point of the series, to derive the
weak formulation of our problem
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"}, we suppose that $u$ is smooth, multiply the
equation by a test function $v \in C_c^\infty(I\times U)$ and integrate
over $I\times U$. We obtain that, in addition to the condition $u(0)=g$
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{weak 0}
  \int_I \int_U u' v + \int_I \int_U (\bm{A} \nabla u) \cdot \nabla v + \int_I \int_U (\bm{b} \cdot \nabla u ) v + \int_I \int_U c u v = \int_I \int_U f v,
\end{aligned}$$ where for brevity in the notation we omitted the
customary $\,\mathrm{d}x\,\mathrm{d}t$ in the integrals and wrote
$u'= \partial _t u$. Equivalently, with the notation for the duality
pairing, we can write [\[weak 0\]](#weak 0){reference-type="eqref"
reference="weak 0"} and the boundary condition as $$\begin{aligned}
\label{weak}
 (v, u')+ (\nabla v, \bm{A} \nabla u) + ( v, \bm{b}\nabla u) + (v, cu) = (v, f), \quad u(0) = g.
\end{aligned}$$ For the following theory we need some assumption on the
coefficients and the data. Firstly, we give the following definition

::: definition
**Definition 1**. *We say that $\bm{A}$ is *parabolic* if there exists a
constant $\alpha>0$ such that $$\begin{aligned}
\label{parabolicity}
 (\bm{A}(t,\bm{x}) \bm{\xi}) \cdot \bm{\xi} \geq \alpha \left| \xi \right|^2 \quad \forall (t,\bm{x}) \in I \times \Omega,\, \bm{\xi} \in \mathbb{R}^d.
  
\end{aligned}$$*
:::

This is the parabolic analog of the ellipticity condition we required in
the previous post. Physically speaking, it guarantees that diffusion
does not go to zero and occurs from regions of larger concentration to
lower concentration.

::: {#ass .assumption}
**Assumption 1**. We assume that $\bm{A}$ is parabolic and for all
$i,j=1,\dots,d$ $$\begin{aligned}
 A_{ij}, b_i , c \in L^\infty_{t,x}, \quad f \in L^2_t H^{-1}_x, \quad g \in L^2_x.
  
\end{aligned}$$
:::

The boundedness of $\bm{A},\bm{b},c$ is expedient so that the integrals
in [\[weak\]](#weak){reference-type="eqref" reference="weak"} are well
defined.

For [\[weak\]](#weak){reference-type="eqref" reference="weak"} to make
sense and verify the boundary condition $u=0$ on $\partial U$, we give
the following definition.

::: {#weak def .definition}
**Definition 2** (Weak solution). *Under Assumption
[1](#ass){reference-type="ref" reference="ass"}, we say that $u$ is a
*weak solution of the parabolic problem*
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"} if $$\begin{aligned}
 u \in L^2_t H^1_{0,x}, \quad u' \in L^2_t H^{-1}_x,
  
\end{aligned}$$ and [\[weak\]](#weak){reference-type="eqref"
reference="weak"} is verified for all $v \in L^2_tH^1_{0,x}$.*
:::

With the above definition, all the terms
[\[weak\]](#weak){reference-type="eqref" reference="weak"} are well
defined, where the duality pairing is to be interpreted as the one given
by [\[duality\]](#duality){reference-type="eqref" reference="duality"}.
Furthermore, the boundary condition $u=0$ on $\partial U$ is
automatically satisfied as $u(t) \in H_0^1(U)$ for almost all $t$ and a
quick sanity check shows that the various functional spaces in
definition [2](#weak def){reference-type="ref" reference="weak def"} are
logical as if $u \in L^2_t H^1_{0,x}$ then
$\mathcal{L}u \in L^2_t H^{-1}_x$ and from
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"} we should also have $u' \in L^2_t H^{-1}_x$. It
only remains to justify that $u(0)$ is well-defined. This is proved in
the following lemma.

::: {#continuity weak .lemma}
**Lemma 3**. *Let $u \in L_t^2H_{x}^1$ with $u' \in L_tH_x^{-1}$. Then,
$u \in C_t L_x^2$ with $$\begin{aligned}
    \left\lVert u \right\rVert_{C_t L_x^2} \lesssim \left\lVert u(0) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}+ \left\lVert u \right\rVert_{L_t^2H_{x}^1}+ \left\lVert u' \right\rVert_{L_tH_x^{-1}}.
  
\end{aligned}$$*
:::

::: proof
*Proof.* Let $\phi_n \in C_c^ \infty(I)$ be a smooth approximation to
the identity and define $u_n(t):=(u* \phi_n)(t)$ (we use the convention
of extending $u$ by zero outside of $I$ so the convolution is well
defined). Then, $u_n \in C^\infty_t L_x^2$ converges almost everywhere
and in $L_t^2 H_x^1$ to $u$ and $u_n '$ converges in $L_t^2 H_x^{-1}$ to
$u'$ (see [\[approximation\]](#approximation){reference-type="eqref"
reference="approximation"}).Given $n , m >0$ write
$w_{n,m}:= u_n - u_m$. We have that, $$\begin{aligned}
    \left(\left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert^2_{L_x^2}\right)' = 2\left\langle u_n(t),u_n'(t)\right\rangle_{L^2_x}.
  
\end{aligned}$$ As a result, by the fundamental theorem of calculus,
given any $s,t \in I$, $$\begin{aligned}
    \left\lVert w_{n,m}(t) \right\rVert_{L^2_x}^2 = \left\lVert w_{n,m}(s) \right\rVert_{L^2_x}^2 +2\int_{s}^t \left\langle w_{n,m}(r),w_{n,m}'(r)\right\rangle_{L^2_x} \,\mathrm{d}r.
  
\end{aligned}$$ Taking any $s$ such that $u_n(s) \to u(s)$ and the max
over $t$ gives $$\begin{aligned}
\label{Cauchy}
    \limsup_{n , m \to \infty} \max_{t \in I}\left\lVert w_{n,m}(t)  \right\rVert_{L^2_x}^2 \lesssim 0 + \limsup_{n , m \to \infty}\int_{I} \left\lVert w_{n,m}(r)  \right\rVert_{H^1_x}^2 \,\mathrm{d}r + \int_{I} \left\lVert w'_{n,m}(r) \right\rVert_{H^{-1}_x}^2 \,\mathrm{d}r=0,
  
\end{aligned}$$ where in the last equality we used that $u_n ,u'_n$
converge to $u,u'$ in the respective norms.

Equation [\[Cauchy\]](#Cauchy){reference-type="eqref"
reference="Cauchy"} shows that $u_n$ is a Cauchy sequence in the Banach
space $C_t L_x^2$ and, as a result, converges to a continuous function
$v$ in $C_t L_x^2$. Since it also converges almost everywhere to $u$, we
must have that $v=u$. This shows that $u \in C_t L_x^2$.

To prove the bound of the theorem, suppose first that $u$ is smooth in
$t$. Then, by the fundamental theorem of calculus and the definition of
the dual norm $$\begin{aligned}
    \left\lVert u(t) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2 & = \left\lVert u(0) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2 + 2\int_I \left\langle u'(r),u(r)\right\rangle_{L_x^2} \,\mathrm{d}r \\&\leq \left\lVert u(0) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2 + 2 \int_I \left\lVert u'(r) \right\rVert_{L^2_tH_x^{-1}}\left\lVert u(r) \right\rVert_{L^2_tH_x^1} \,\mathrm{d}r.
  
\end{aligned}$$ The bound follows Cauchy-Schwartz, and the non-smooth
case follows by approximating $u$ by smooth $u_n = u*phi_n$. ◻
:::

# Well-posedness of the problem

We now aim to show that the problem
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"}, or more precisely its weak formulation
[\[weak\]](#weak){reference-type="eqref" reference="weak"}, is
well-posed.

## A naive approach

As we have discussed in the previous section, equation
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"} can be seen as an infinite dimensional linear
ODE. As a result, we could hope that the theory of linear ODE will give
us a solution. Working directly we would write
$F(u):= -\mathcal{L}u + f$ and the equation
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"} as $$\begin{aligned}
\label{ode}
 u'(t)= F(u(t)), \quad u(0) = g.
\end{aligned}$$ Then, writing once more $X =H_0^1(U)$, we could try to
emulate Picard's theorem for scalar-valued ODEs to obtain a fixed point
for $$\begin{aligned}
  \Phi: C([0,\epsilon ] \to X) \to C([0,\epsilon ] \to X), \quad u \mapsto \Phi(u)(t) := g + \int_I F(u(s)) \,\mathrm{d}s.
\end{aligned}$$ The only problem with this is that $F$ does not map $X$
to $X$. As a result, the mapping $\Phi$ is not well defined. If the
initial data is smooth, we could hope to set $X = C_c^\infty(U)$, and
then $F$ would map $X$ to $X$. However, $C^\infty_c(U)$ is not a Banach
space, so further modifications would be required. As a result, we need
a more refined approach. In the next section, we use the Galerkin method
to prove the problem's well-posedness.

## Galerkin solutions

Instead of working directly in infinite dimensions, we project our
problem onto a finite-dimensional space spanned by $n$ basis functions
$\left\{\phi_i\right\}_{i=1}^n$. If we are able to solve the projected
problem, we hope that as the number of basis functions $n$ increases,
the solution will converge to the true solution. This is the idea behind
the Galerkin method, which is widely used in the numerical study of
PDEs.

::: exercise
**Exercise 1**. Use that $L^2(U)$ is separable to show that it has a
smooth orthonormal basis of functions
$\left\{\phi_i\right\}_{i=1}^\infty$. that is, $$\begin{aligned}
  \left\langle\phi_i, \phi_j\right\rangle_{L^2(U)} = \delta_{ij}, \quad \phi_i \in C^\infty_c(U).
\end{aligned}$$
:::

::: hint
**Hint 1**. Since $L^2(U)$ is separable, it has a countable dense subset
$\left\{f_i\right\}_{i=1}^\infty \subset L^2(U)$. Since $C_c^\infty(U)$
is dense in $L^2(U)$, for each $f_i$ there exists a sequence
$\left\{\phi_{i,n}\right\}_{n=1}^\infty \subset C_c^\infty(U)$ that
converges to $f_i$ in $L^2(U)$. Then, the set
$\left\{\phi_{i,n}\right\}_{i,n}$ is a countable dense subset of
$L^2(U)$, and we can apply the Gram-Schmidt process to obtain an
orthonormal basis.

Alternatively, if $U$ is bounded and smooth, $\Delta ^{-1}$ is compact
and self-adjoint. Hence, it has a countable orthonormal basis of
eigenfunctions which are smooth by the [previous
post](https://nowheredifferentiable.com/2024-02-28-PDEs-6-Elliptic_PDE._Well_posedness_and_regularity/#:~:text=for%20smooth%20coefficients.-,Theorem%2016)
and induction.
:::

Let $\left\{\phi_i\right\}_{i=1}^\infty\subset C^\infty_c(U)$ be an
orthonormal basis of $L^2(U)$, let
$V_n:= \text{span}\left\{\phi_i\right\}_{i=1}^n$ and let
$$\begin{aligned}
  \mathcal{S}_n:= C(I \to V_n)= \left\{\sum_{j=1}^n \lambda _j(t) \phi_j: \lambda_j \in C(I)\right\}.
\end{aligned}$$ Consider the problem of finding $u_n \in \mathcal{S}_n$
such that, for all $i=1,....,n$ and $t \in I$ $$\begin{aligned}
\label{galerkin}
  \left\langle\phi_i, u'_n(t)\right\rangle_{L^2_x} + B(\phi_i,u_n(t);t) & = (\phi _i, f(t)),\quad
 \left\langle\phi_i, u_n(0)\right\rangle_{L^2_x}           = \left\langle\phi_i,g\right\rangle_{L_x^2},
\end{aligned}$$ where $\left\langle\cdot ,\cdot \right\rangle_{L^2_x}$
denotes the inner product in $L^2_x$, $(\cdot,\cdot)$ is the pairing of
an element in $H^1_{x,0}$ with an element in its dual and
$B(\cdot ,\cdot ,t)$ is the bilinear form on $H^1_0(U)$ defined by
$$\begin{aligned}
 B(w,v; t) := \int_U \bm{A}(t) \nabla v \cdot \nabla w + (\bm{b}(t) \cdot \nabla v) w + c(t) v w \,\mathrm{d}x .
\end{aligned}$$ Equation
[\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref" reference="galerkin"}
is known as the *Galerkin problem*.

::: theorem
**Theorem 4** (Well-posedness of the Galerkin problem). *Under
Assumption [1](#ass){reference-type="ref" reference="ass"}, the Galerkin
problem [\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref"
reference="galerkin"} is well-posed. That is, a unique solution $u_n$
exists and depends continuously on the initial data. Furthermore,
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{bound}
    \left\lVert u_n \right\rVert_{C_t L_x^2} +\left\lVert u_n \right\rVert_{L^2_tH_{x}^1} + \left\lVert u_n' \right\rVert_{L^2_tH_{x}^{-1}} \lesssim_{\bm{A,b},c,T} \left\lVert f \right\rVert_{L^2_t H_x^{-1}}+ \left\lVert g \right\rVert_{L_x^2}.
  
\end{aligned}$$*
:::

::: proof
*Proof.* We divide the proof into three parts. Existence, continuity,
and uniqueness.

a)  Existence of solutions: Since the $\phi_i$ are orthonormal and we
    impose $u_n \in \mathcal{S}_n$, solving
    [\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="galerkin"} is equivalent to finding
    $\bm{\lambda}_n\in C(I \to \mathbb{R}^n)$ such that
    $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{galerkin 2}
             \bm{\lambda}_n'(t) + \bm{B}_n(t) \bm{\lambda}_n(t) = \bm{f}_n(t), \quad \bm{\lambda}_n(0) = \bm{g}_n,
           
    \end{aligned}$$ where we define the matrix
    $\bm{B}_n(t) \in R^{n \times n}$ and vectors
    $\bm{f}_n(t), \bm{g}_n \in \mathbb{R}^n$ as $$\begin{aligned}
    _{ij}:= B(\phi_i, \phi_j;t), \quad [\bm{f}_n(t)]_i:= (\phi_i, f(t)), \quad [\bm{g_n}]_i:= \left\langle\phi_i,g\right\rangle_{L_x^2}.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ The equivalence of solving
    [\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="galerkin"} and
    [\[galerkin 2\]](#galerkin 2){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="galerkin 2"} is obtained by setting
    $u_n(t) = \sum_{j=1}^n [\bm{\lambda}_n]_j \phi_j$.

    Since $\phi _i \in C_c^\infty(U)$, by the boundedness of the
    coefficients in Assumption [1](#ass){reference-type="ref"
    reference="ass"}, and by the construction of $\bm{B}_n,\bm{f}_n$. It
    holds that $\bm{B}_n \in L^\infty_t$ and $\bm{f}_n \in L^2_t$. As a
    result, according to standard ODE theory, there exists a unique
    continuous solution $\bm{\lambda }$ to
    [\[galerkin 2\]](#galerkin 2){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="galerkin 2"}. One can even write out the explicit
    expression for $\bm{\lambda }$ using Duhamel's formula,
    $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{duhamel}
             \bm{\lambda}_n(t) = e^{\int_0^t \bm{B}_n(s) \,\mathrm{d}s } \bm{g}_n + \int_0^t e^{ \int_{s}^t \bm{B}_n(r) \,\mathrm{d}r} \bm{f}_n(s) \,\mathrm{d}s.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ This proves existence.

b)  Continuity in the data: The plan will be to apply Gronwall's
    inequality. By [\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="galerkin"} and the linearity of the inner product, we
    obtain $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{sol un}
             \left\langle u_n(t),u_n'(t)\right\rangle_{L^2_x}+B(u_n(t),u_n(t);t) = (u_n(t), f(t)).
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Now, since $u_n$ is smooth and by differentiating
    under the integral sign, $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{derivative}
             \left\langle u_n(t),u_n'(t)\right\rangle_{L^2_x} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2\right)'.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Using Cauchy's inequality
    $ab \leq \frac{1}{2} (\epsilon a^2+\epsilon^{-1} b^2)$, the
    boundedness of the coefficients and the ellipticity of $\bm{A}$
    shows that, for some constants $\beta , \nu >0$ $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{B bound}
     B(u_n(t),u_n(t);t) \geq \gamma \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H^1_x}^2 - \nu\left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{L^2_x}^2
           
    \end{aligned}$$ (this is the same as [what was
    proved](https://nowheredifferentiable.com/2024-02-28-PDEs-6-Elliptic_PDE._Well_posedness_and_regularity/#:~:text=%20.%20We-,recall,-the%20Cauchy%20inequality)
    as in the elliptic case). Whereas, by definition of the dual and by
    Cauchy's inequality, the bound of the right-hand side of
    [\[sol un\]](#sol un){reference-type="eqref" reference="sol un"} is,
    $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{rhs bound}
             \left| (u_n(t), f(t)) \right| \leq \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H_{x}^1} \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert_{H_x^{-1}}\leq \frac{1}{2} \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H_{x}^1}^2 + \frac{1}{2} \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert_{H_x^{-1}}^2.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Using
    [\[derivative\]](#derivative){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="derivative"},
    [\[B bound\]](#B bound){reference-type="eqref" reference="B bound"}
    and [\[rhs bound\]](#rhs bound){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="rhs bound"} in equation
    [\[sol un\]](#sol un){reference-type="eqref" reference="sol un"} we
    obtain that $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{i0}
             \left(\left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2\right)' + \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H^1_x}^2 \lesssim \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert^2_{L_x^2} + \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert_{ H_x^{-1}}^2.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ In particular, $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{i00}
             \left(\left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2\right)' \lesssim \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert^2_{L_x^2} + \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert_{H_x^{-1}}^2.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Given differentiable $v:I \to \mathbb{R}$ aa
    constants $\alpha\in \mathbb{R}$ and integrable $\beta \in L^1(I)$,
    Gronwall's inequality states that $$\begin{aligned}
     v'(t) \leq \alpha v(t) + \beta \quad \Rightarrow \quad v(t) \leq e^{\alpha t}v(0) + \int_I e^{\alpha(t-s)}\beta(s)\,\mathrm{d}s.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Applying this to
    [\[i00\]](#i00){reference-type="eqref" reference="i00"} gives
    $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{i1}
             \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2 \lesssim e^{\alpha t}\left(\left\lVert g \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2 + \int_I \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert^2_{ H_x^{-1}}\,\mathrm{d}t\right) \lesssim \left\lVert g \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2 + \left\lVert f \right\rVert_{L^2_t H_x^{-1}}^2.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Taking the maximum in
    [\[i1\]](#i1){reference-type="eqref" reference="i1"} gives the first
    part of the bound in [\[bound\]](#bound){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="bound"} $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{ub}
             \left\lVert u_n \right\rVert^2_{C_t L_x^2} \lesssim \left\lVert g \right\rVert^2_{L_x^2} + \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert^2_{L^2_t H_x^{-1}}.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ To bound the second term in
    [\[bound\]](#bound){reference-type="eqref" reference="bound"} we
    combine [\[i1\]](#i1){reference-type="eqref" reference="i1"} with
    [\[i0\]](#i0){reference-type="eqref" reference="i0"} to obtain
    $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{hb0}
             \left(\left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2\right)' + \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H^1_x}^2 \lesssim \left\lVert g \right\rVert^2_{L_x^2} + \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert^2_{H_x^{-1}}.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Integrating over $I$ in
    [\[hb0\]](#hb0){reference-type="eqref" reference="hb0"} and applying
    the fundamental theorem of calculus together with
    [\[ub\]](#ub){reference-type="eqref" reference="ub"} gives
    $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{hb}
             \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{L_t^2H^1_x}^2:= \int_I \left\lVert u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H^1_x}^2 \,\mathrm{d}t \lesssim \left\lVert g \right\rVert^2_{L_x^2} + \left\lVert f \right\rVert^2_{L^2_t H_x^{-1}}.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ To bound $u_n'$ consider any $v \in H_{0,x}^1$, and
    write $v=v_n+v_n^\perp$ where $v_n$ is the projection of $v$ onto
    $V_n$ with the inner product on $H^1_x$. Since $v_n^\perp$ is
    orthogonal to $u_n'(t)\in V_n$ for each $t$, from
    [\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="galerkin"} we have $$\begin{aligned}
             \left\langle u_n'(t),v\right\rangle_{L^2_x} & = \left\langle u_n'(t),v_n\right\rangle_{L^2_x}= (f(t),v_n)- B(u_n(t),v_n;t)                                                                \\
                        & \lesssim \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert_{H_x^{-1}}\left\lVert v_n(t) \right\rVert_{H^1_x} + \left\lVert  u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H^1_x}\left\lVert v_n \right\rVert_{H^1_x} \lesssim \left(\left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert_{H_x^{-1}} + \left\lVert  u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H^1_x}\right)\left\lVert v \right\rVert_{H^1_x},
           
    \end{aligned}$$ where we used that
    $\left\lVert v_n \right\rVert_{H^1_x}= \left\lVert v \right\rVert_{H^1_x}- \left\lVert v_n^\perp \right\rVert_{H^1_x} \leq \left\lVert v \right\rVert_{H^1_x}$.
    We deduce that $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{i2}
             \left\lVert u_n'(t) \right\rVert_{H^{-1}_x} \lesssim \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert_{H_x^{-1}} + \left\lVert  u_n(t) \right\rVert_{H^1_x}.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Integrating the square over $I$ and using
    [\[hb\]](#hb){reference-type="eqref" reference="hb"} in
    [\[i2\]](#i2){reference-type="eqref" reference="i2"} gives
    $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{i3}
             \left\lVert u_n'(t) \right\rVert^2_{L_t^2H^{-1}_x} \lesssim \left\lVert f(t) \right\rVert^2_{L^2_t H_x^{-1}} + \left\lVert g \right\rVert^2_{L_x^2}.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Now, combining [\[ub\]](#ub){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="ub"} and [\[hb\]](#hb){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="hb"}, [\[i3\]](#i3){reference-type="eqref"
    reference="i3"} and taking square roots, we obtain the desired bound
    [\[bound\]](#bound){reference-type="eqref" reference="bound"}.

c)  Uniqueness: To conclude uniqueness, let $u_n^{{1}}, u_n^{{2}}$ be
    two solutions to the Galerkin problem. Then,
    $w_n := u_n^{{1}}-u_n^{{2}}$ verifies the homogeneous problem
    $$\begin{aligned}
             \left\langle w_n',v\right\rangle_{L^2_x} + B(w_n,v;t) = 0, \quad \forall v \in V_n, \quad \text{and} \quad w_n(0) = 0.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ The same reasoning that proved
    [\[i00\]](#i00){reference-type="eqref" reference="i00"}, where now
    $f=0$, shows that $$\begin{aligned}
             \left(\left\lVert w_n(t) \right\rVert_{L_x^2}^2\right)' \lesssim \left\lVert w_n(t) \right\rVert^2_{L_x^2}.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ Now, applying Grönwall's inequality and by the
    initial condition $w_n(0)=0$, we obtain for some constant $C>0$
    $$\begin{aligned}
    \label{w0}
             \left\lVert w_n(t) \right\rVert_{L_x^2} \leq e^{Ct} \left\lVert w_n(0) \right\rVert_{L_x^2} = 0.
           
    \end{aligned}$$ This shows that $w_n=0$ and hence
    $u_n^{{1}}=u_n^{{2}}$. This proves uniqueness and concludes the
    proof.

:::

Having proved the well-posedness of the Galerkin problem, we can now
show that the parabolic problem is well-posed. A common technique in PDE
is to modify your initial problem $P$ by some quantity $\epsilon$ to
obtain a problem $P_ \epsilon$ that is easier to solve. Suppose one can
find solutions to $P_ \epsilon$ that are bounded. In that case, a
converging subsequence can typically be extracted and, under appropriate
conditions, will converge to a solution to the original problem $P$.
This is exactly what we show now.

::: theorem
**Theorem 5** (Well posedness of the parabolic problem). *Under
Assumption [1](#ass){reference-type="ref" reference="ass"}, the
parabolic problem [\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"} is well-posed. That is, there exists a unique
weak solution $u$, which depends continuously on the initial data with
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{bound P}
    \left\lVert u \right\rVert_{C_t L_x^2} +\left\lVert u \right\rVert_{L^2_tH_{x}^1} + \left\lVert u' \right\rVert_{L^2_tH_{x}^{-1}} \lesssim_{\bm{A,b},c,T} \left\lVert f \right\rVert_{L^2_t H_x^{-1}}+ \left\lVert g \right\rVert_{L_x^2}.
  
\end{aligned}$$*
:::

::: proof
*Proof.* Let $\left\{u_n\right\}_{n=1}^\infty$ be the sequence of
solutions to the Galerkin problem
[\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref" reference="galerkin"}
guaranteed by Theorem [\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref"
reference="galerkin"}. By said theorem, $u_n$ and $u'_n$ are bounded
sequences in $L_t^2H_{0,x}^1$ and $L_t^2 H_x^{-1}$ respectively. Since
these spaces are Hilbert spaces, they are reflexive, and we deduce by
the [Banach-Alaoglu
theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach%E2%80%93Alaoglu_theorem)
that respective subsequences converge in their respective spaces to some
$u \in L_tH_{0,x}^1$ and $\widetilde{u} \in L_t H_x^{-1}$. That is,
$$\begin{aligned}
 u= \lim_{k \to \infty}u_{n_k} \in L_t^2H_{0,x}^1, \quad \widetilde{u}= \lim_{k \to \infty}u_{n_k} \in L_t^2 H_x^{-1}.
  
\end{aligned}$$ We first show that $\widetilde{u} = u'$. We have that,
given $\phi \in C_c^\infty(I \times U)$ $$\begin{aligned}
 (\phi,\widetilde{u}')\lim_{k \to \infty} \left(\phi, u_{n_k}'\right) & = \lim_{k \to \infty} \left(-\phi', u_{n_k}\right) = \left(-\phi', u\right) ,
  
\end{aligned}$$ where in the first equality we used the weak convergence
of $u'_{n_k}$ to $\widetilde{u}$ in $L_t H_x^{-1}$, in the second
equality we used the definition of weak derivative, and in the last we
used the convergence of $u_{n_k}$ to $u$ in $L_t^2H_{0,x}^1$. This shows
that $u' = \widetilde{u}$ almost everywhere. We now show that $u$ solves
the weak problem [\[weak\]](#weak){reference-type="eqref"
reference="weak"}. By construction of the Galerkin solutions in
[\[galerkin\]](#galerkin){reference-type="eqref" reference="galerkin"}
and integrating over $I$, we deduce for any $v \in \mathcal{S}_{n_k}$
$$\begin{aligned}
\label{weak k}
 (v, u'_{n_k})+ (\nabla v, \bm{A} \nabla u_{n_k}) + (\nabla v, \bm{b} u_{n_k}) + (v, cu_{n_k}) = (v, f).
  
\end{aligned}$$ As a result, taking limits in $k$ shows that, for all
$v \in \mathcal{S}_n$ $$\begin{aligned}
 (v, u')+ (\nabla v, \bm{A} \nabla u) + (\nabla v, \bm{b} u) + (v, cu) = (v, f).
  
\end{aligned}$$ Since the space $\mathcal{S}_n$ is dense in
$L_t^2H_{0,x}^1$ we deduce that $$\begin{aligned}
 (v, u')+ (\nabla v, \bm{A} \nabla u) + (\nabla v, \bm{b} u) + (v, cu) = (v, f), \quad \forall v \in L_t^2H_{0,x}^1.
  
\end{aligned}$$ We now check that $u(0)=g$. To do so, we now consider
$v \in C^1_tH_{0,x}^1 \cap \mathcal{S}_{n_k}$ such that $v(T)=0$. Then,
integrating by parts over $I$ and using
[\[weak k\]](#weak k){reference-type="eqref" reference="weak k"} gives
$$\begin{aligned}
    & -(v', u_{n_k}) + (\nabla v, \bm{A} \nabla u_{n_k}) + (\nabla v, \bm{b} u_{n_k}) + (v, cu_{n_k}) \\&= (v, f)+  \left\langle v(0),g\right\rangle_{L_x^2}=(v, f)+  \left\langle v(0),u_{n_k}(0)\right\rangle_{L_x^2}.
  
\end{aligned}$$ Taking limits above and by density of $\mathcal{S}_n$ in
$C_t^2H_{0,x}^1$ we obtain that $$\begin{aligned}
    \left\langle v(0),g\right\rangle_{L_x^2} = \left\langle v(0),u(0)\right\rangle_{L_x^2}, \quad \forall v \in C_t^2H_{0,x}^1.
  
\end{aligned}$$ where we used that by Lemma
[3](#continuity weak){reference-type="ref" reference="continuity weak"}
$u_{n_k} \to u \in C_t L_x^2$. In particular, $$\begin{aligned}
    \left\langle w,g\right\rangle_{L_x^2} = \left\langle w,u(0)\right\rangle_{L_x^2}, \quad \forall w \in H_{0,x}^1.
  
\end{aligned}$$ Since $H_{0,x}^1$ is dense in $L_x^2$ this shows that
$u(0)=g$ and concludes the proof. ◻
:::

::: observation
**Observation 1**. It may seem like the conclusion is impossible. After
all, we did not impose that $\left.g\right|_{U}=0$. So how can we hope
that for $u(t)$ to be $0$ on $\partial U$ if $u(0)=g$? The solution lies
in the fact that $u \in C_t L_{x}^2 \cap L_t^2H_{0,x}^1$, but it may not
hold that $u \in C_tH_{0,x}^1$. As a result, we only know that
$u(t) \in H_0^1(U)$ for almost every $t$. And it is not required that
$u(t) \in H_0^1(U)$ for $t=0$.
:::

# Regularity of the solutions

Having proved the well-posedness of the parabolic problem
[\[parabolic\]](#parabolic){reference-type="eqref"
reference="parabolic"} and its finite-dimensional Galerkin
approximation, we can now move on to study the regularity of the
solutions. The proof is similar to the one given in the [elliptic
case](https://nowheredifferentiable.com/2024-02-28-PDEs-6-Elliptic_PDE._Well_posedness_and_regularity/)
but more technical, and we will mainly cite some main results without
proof.

As one expects from the elliptic case, the spatial regularity of the
solutions is increased by $2$. Our study of the previous section shows
that the regularity of the time derivatives of the solution is $2$
orders lower. In fact, it is two orders lower for each time derivative
taken. To be able to obtain higher regularity however, we will need to
impose some compatibility between the boundary condition $f$ and the
initial data $g$. The following can be found in Section 7.13 of
[@evans2022partial]

::: theorem
**Theorem 6**. *Let $\Omega$ be a bounded domain with smooth boundary
$\partial \Omega$. Let $\mathcal{L}$ be the elliptic operator in
[\[elliptic operator\]](#elliptic operator){reference-type="eqref"
reference="elliptic operator"} and assume that the coefficients
$\bm{A,b},c$ are independent of time $t$ and smooth in space. Suppose
that $$\begin{aligned}
 g \in H^{2 k+1}_x,\quad \text{and} \quad  \frac{\partial ^m f}{\partial t^m} \in L^2_tH_x^{2 k-2 m} \quad \text{for } m=0,...,k,
  
\end{aligned}$$ and the compatibility conditions $$\begin{aligned}
 g_0:=g \in H_{x,0}^1 \quad g_1:=f(0)-\mathcal{L}g_0 \in H_{x,0}^1 \quad \ldots\quad g_j:=\frac{\partial ^{m-1} f}{\partial t^{m-1}}(0)-\mathcal{L}g_{m-1} \in H_{x,0}^1
  
\end{aligned}$$ are satisfied. Then, $$\begin{aligned}
  \frac{\partial ^m u}{\partial t^m} \in L^2_tH_x^{2 k+2-2 m} \quad \text{for } m=0,...,k+1,
  
\end{aligned}$$ and we have the estimate $$\begin{aligned}
  \sum_{m=0}^{k+1} \left\lVert \frac{\partial^m u}{\partial t^m} \right\rVert_{L^2_tH_x^{2 k+2-2 m}}\lesssim \sum_{m=0}^k \left\lVert \frac{\partial ^m f}{\partial t^m} \right\rVert_{L^2_tH_x^{2 k-2 m}}+\left\lVert g \right\rVert_{H_x^{2 k+1}}.
  
\end{aligned}$$ In consequence, if
$f \in C^\infty_{t,x}, g \in C^\infty_x$ then $u \in C^\infty_{t,x}$.*
:::

We also show a result where the coefficients are allowed to depend on
time. The following can be found in Chapter $3$ of
[@Friedman1983PartialDE] and assumes that the coefficients are [Hölder
continuous](https://nowheredifferentiable.com/2023-07-12-PDEs-3-Sobolev_spaces/#:~:text=%2C%20we-,define,-the%20Holder%20space)

::: theorem
**Theorem 7**. *Let $\Omega$ be a bounded domain with smooth boundary
and assume that for all $t \in I$,
$A_{ij}(t),b_j(t), c_j(t) \in C^{k, \alpha}(\Omega )$. If $u$ is a
classical solution of $\mathcal{L}u=f$, then
$u(t) \in C^{k+2,\alpha}(\Omega ), u'(t) \in C^{k, \alpha}(\Omega )$ for
all $t \in I$. In consequence, if
$f \in C^\infty_{t,x}, g \in C^\infty_x$ then $u \in C^\infty_{t,x}$.*
:::
