PDEs 3: Sobolev spaces

Pdes
Density, trace and embeddings
Author

Liam Llamazares–Elias

Published

July 12, 2023

1 Three line summary

  • Given open \(U \subset \R^d\) the Sobolev spaces \(W^{k,p}(U)\) are complete spaces of ** weakly differentiable** functions.

  • Smooth functions are dense in \(W^{k,p}(\Omega)\) if \(\Omega\) is bounded and has \(C^1\) boundary. This lets us manipulate Sobolev functions as if they were smooth and then take limits. In particular, we can extend and restrict functions to \(\partial \Omega\).

  • Functions in \(W^{k,p}(\Omega)\) enjoy various inequalities. Cashing in differentiability for integrability we can compactly embed

    \[ \begin{aligned} W^{k,p}(\Omega) \hookrightarrow L^q(\Omega). \end{aligned} \]

    Where \(q\equiv q(d,k,p)\) decreases with the dimension \(d\), increases with differentiability \(k\) and integrability \(p\), and is larger than \(p\).

2 Why should I care?

Sobolev spaces allow us to extend the notion of differentiability to a wider class of functions. The fact that these spaces are complete and compactly embedded in \(L^q\) spaces is an important tool to extract convergent sub-sequences. This is useful when solving differential equations as a common technique is to take a Cauchy sequence whose limit is the solution to the equation.

3 Notation

  • In this post we will be dealing with functions over a variety of domains. To facilitate interpretation of the notation we will stick to the convention that \(K\) is a compact set, \(U, V\) are open sets, and \(\Omega\) is an open bounded set with \(C^1\) boundary.

  • Given a topological space \(X\) and a subset \(A \subset X\) we abbreviate \(A\) is dense in \(X\) with the topology of \(X\) by

    \[ \begin{aligned} \overline{A}=X. \end{aligned} \]

    We stress that in practice \(X\) may be itself a subset of some larger space \(Y\) (for example \(X= H^s(\R^d)\) and \(Y= L^p(\R^d)\)) . However, the above notation will always mean the closure with the topology of \(X\) and not \(Y\).

  • A related notation is we will write given \(a_n \in A\)

    \[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{n \to \infty}a_n =x \in X. \end{aligned} \]

    To mean the limit in the topology of \(X\).

  • Given two sets \(A,B\) we write \(A \Subset B\) and say that \(A\) is compactly included in \(B\) if \(\overline{A}\) is compact and strictly included in \(B\).

  • We also write

    \[ \begin{aligned} A+B=\set{x+y:x \in A, y \in B} . \end{aligned} \]

  • Given a topological vector space \(X\) we write \(X'\) for the dual of \(X\) and denote given \(w \in X', \varphi \in X\) the duality pairing as

    \[ \begin{aligned} (\varphi,w):= w(\varphi) . \end{aligned} \]

  • We will always write \(\alpha\) for a multi-index \(\alpha \in \N^d\) and use the notation

    \[ \begin{aligned} D^\alpha f := \partial _1^{\alpha_1}\cdots \partial _d^{\alpha_d}. \end{aligned} \]

    In the case \(\alpha =0\) we use the convention \(D^0 =f\).

  • Given a space of functions \(X\) with domain \(A\) and \(B \subset A\) we write

    \[ \begin{aligned} \restr{X}{B}:=\set{\restr{f}{B}: f\in X} . \end{aligned} \]

  • Given two quantities \(M,N\) we write \(M \lesssim N\) to mean that there exists some constant \(C\) independent of \(M\) and \(N\) such that \(M \leq C N\).

  • We write \(B_r(x)\) for the ball centered at \(x\) with radius \(r\) and \(B_r\) if \(x=0\). The space where the ball is contained depending on context.

4 Introduction

Hi everyone, welcome back to another post on our series on PDEs. This post goes into some depth on the main space of functions we will work with. Since the post is rather long (yes it has appendices, no that wasn’t planned) I decided to include a pdf at the end of the post in case it is easier to navigate. That said, in the future we will want to solve a differential equation of the form

\[ \begin{aligned} \Ll u = f\quad \mathrm{ on }\quad D . \end{aligned} \tag{1}\]

Where \(D\) is some domain in \(\R^d\). In a previous post on the Fourier transform we saw how to define the Sobolev spaces \(H^s(D)\) when \(D\) is the whole Euclidean space \(\R^d\) or the torus \(\T^d\). These spaces correspond to \(s\)-times weakly differentiable functions and we saw how these spaces could help us solve (1). However, in practice \(D\) may be an open set in \(\R^d\) or even some \(d\)-dimensional manifold with a boundary condition

\[ \begin{aligned} \restr{u}{\partial D}= g. \end{aligned} \tag{2}\]

Note that equation (2) is a priori ill-defined as the Lebesgue measure of \(\partial D \subset \R^d\) is zero. Thus, it is necessary to extend the theory to a wider class of domains and to explain what we mean by the restriction of a function to its boundary of definition.

4.1 A first attempt

Suppose for example \(D=U\) is an open set and \(u: U \to \R\). Then, we can try to define \(H^s(U)\) using our knowledge of \(H^s(\R^d)\) by:

  1. Extending \(u\) by zero outside of \(U\) to form

    \[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{u}(x):=\begin{cases} u(x) & \quad x \in U \\ 0 & \quad x \not\in U \end{cases}. \end{aligned} \]

  2. Studying if \(\tilde{u} \in H^s(\R^d)\). That is, as we saw in the previous post, checking if

    \[ \begin{aligned} \norm{\tilde{u}}_{H^s(\R^d)}^2= \int_{\R^d}\br{\xi }^{2s}\wh{\tilde{u}}(\xi )^2 \d \xi < \infty \end{aligned} \]

  3. Saying that \(u \in H^s(U)\) if and only if \(\tilde{u} \in H^s(\R^d)\).

However, this runs into problems as is shown in the following example:

Example 1 Let \(U=(0,1)\) and take \(u: (0,1) \to \R\) defined to be identically equal to \(1\). Note that \(u \in C^\infty(U)\) so we expect that \(u \in H^s((0,1))\) for all \(s \in \R\). However, it holds that \(\tilde{u}= {1}_{(0,1)}\) with

\[ \begin{aligned} \wh{\tilde{u}}(\xi )=\frac{1-e^{-2 \pi i \xi }}{2 \pi i \xi }. \end{aligned} \]

As we can see, by substituting in our naive definition of \(H^s(U)\) gives that \(u \in H^s((0,1))\) if and only if \(s< \frac{1}{2}\). Thus, our program of extending \(u\) by zero and studying the regularity of the extension is not going to work. The reason for this is that, by extending by zero we introduce a discontinuity on \(\tilde{u}\) at the boundary of \(U\).

4.2 A second approach

Alternatively, we could also define

\[ \begin{aligned} H^s(U)= \restr{H^s(\R^d)}{U}:= \set{\restr{f}{u}: f \in H^s(\R^d)} . \end{aligned} \]

As we will see later (Exercise 20) this is a better approach. However, it is not optimal as it requires some conditions on \(U\). For example, if \(U\) is not smooth we cannot assert that \(\restr{C^\infty(\R^d)}{U}= C^\infty(U)\).

5 Test functions and distributions

5.1 Seminorms and their topologies

We need a new approach. Motivated as in the previous post by duality we should begin by defining what is meant by a weak derivative of a function \(u\) in \(L^p(U)\). If \(u,\varphi\) are smooth functions then we have by integration by parts

\[ \begin{aligned} (\varphi, D^\alpha u):= \int_{D} \varphi D^\alpha u = (-1)^{\abs{\alpha} }\int_{D}(D^{\alpha} \varphi) u + \text{ boundary effects} . \end{aligned} \]

In the previous post, we used that

  • If \(D =\R^d\) we can take as our test functions \(\varphi \in \Ss(\R^d)\) and use that \(\varphi\) multiplied by any function in \(L^2\) (\(u\) and its derivatives) vanish at infinity to get rid of the boundary effects.

  • If \(D =\T^d\) we can take as our test functions \(\varphi \in C^\infty(\T^d)\) as the boundary effect of periodic functions cancels out.

To obtain this cancellation on a general open \(D\) we need to impose that our test function \(\varphi\) vanishes in a neighborhood of the boundary. That is we need our test functions to have compact support.

Definition 1 Let \(U\) be an open set, then we define \(C_c^\infty(U)\) to be the space of smooth functions whose support is some compact set \(K \subset U\).

Another notation for \(C^\infty_c(U)\) is \(\Dd(U)\) and it is often called the space of test functions for reasons we will later see. Given a compact subset \(K \subset U\) we define for each \(k \in \N\)

Definition 2 Given a compact subset \(K\) of an open set \(U\) we define

\[ \begin{aligned} C_c^\infty(K):= \set{\varphi \in C_c^\infty(U): \supp{\varphi} \subset K } . \end{aligned} \]

We endow \(C_c^\infty(K)\) with the topology generated by the countable family of seminorms

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{\varphi}_{C^k(K)}:= \sup_{x \in K} \sum_{\abs{\alpha}\leq k } \abs{D^\alpha \varphi}, \quad k \in \N_{0} \end{aligned} \tag{3}\]

Later we will need to generate topologies when the family of seminorms is uncountable.

Definition 3 Let \(X\) be a vector space and let \(\mathcal{P}\) be a family of seminorms on \(X\). Then we define \(\tau_\Pp\), the topology generated by \(\mathcal{P}\),

\[ \begin{aligned} B^\rho(x,\eps):=x+\rho^{-1}(B_\eps), \quad \rho \in \Pp, \eps >0 \end{aligned} \]

That is, \(\set{B^\rho(x,\eps): \rho \in \Pp, \eps>0}\) is a sub-basis of \(\tau_\Pp\). Equivalently, \(U\) is open if and only if it is an arbitrary union of finite intersections.

The above topology is equivalent to the initial topology generated by the family

\[ \begin{aligned} \set{\rho(\cdot-x): x\in X, \rho \in \Pp}. \end{aligned} \]

Using the smooth topology we can now work with the dual of \(C_c^\infty(U)\)

Definition 4 We define the space of distributions to be

\[ \begin{aligned} \Dd'(U):= (C_c^\infty(U))'. \end{aligned} \]

And give it the weak-\(\star\) topology.

Remark 2. The inclusions

\[ \begin{aligned} C_c^\infty(U) \hookrightarrow C_c^\infty(\R^d) \hookrightarrow \Ss(\R^d) \end{aligned} \]

are continuous. As a result, \(\Ss'(\R^d) \hookrightarrow \Dd'(U)\). That is, distributions are a larger or more general class than tempered distributions.

Of course, not all smooth functions have compact support. Other (in this case Fréchet) spaces of smooth functions are

\[ \begin{aligned} C^\infty(U) & :=\set{\varphi: \norm{\varphi}_j:= \sup_{\abs{\alpha} \leq j} \norm{D^\alpha \varphi}_{L^\infty(U)}<\infty,\quad \forall k \in \mathbb{N} } \\ C^\infty_{\mathrm{loc}}(U) & :=\set{\varphi: \norm{\varphi}_{k,K}:= \sup_{\abs{\alpha} \leq k} \norm{D^\alpha \varphi}_{L^\infty(K)}<\infty,\quad \forall k \in \mathbb{N}, K \subset U } \end{aligned} \]

Where, we give them respectively the topologies generated by \(\norm{\cdot}_k\) and \(\norm{\cdot}_{k,K}\).

An equivalent characterization of \(\Dd'(U)\) (Taylor 2013, 116:241) is that, \(\omega \in \Dd'(U)\) if and only if for all \(\varphi \in C_c^\infty(U)\) the operator \(\varphi w\) defined by \[ \begin{aligned} (f, \varphi w):= (\varphi f, w), \quad\forall f \in C^\infty(U), \end{aligned} \tag{4}\]

is continuous on \(C^\infty(U)\).

5.2 Locally integrable functions as distributions

In our post on the Fourier transform we saw that integrable functions could naturally be considered as tempered distributions. The analogous is true for distributions. In this case, since our test functions are compactly supported, the functions we pair them up with only need to be locally integrable. We recall the definition

Definition 5 Given \(p\in [1,\infty]\) we write \(L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) for the space of locally \(p\) integrable functions,

\[ \begin{aligned} L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(U):= \set{f: \norm{f}_{L^p(K)}< \infty, \,\text{ for all compact } K \subset U}. \end{aligned} \]

And endow it with the topology generated by the family of seminorms

\[ \begin{aligned} \rho_{K_n,p}(f):= \norm{f}_{L^p(K_n)}. \end{aligned} \]

Where \(K_n\) are selected such that \(\bigcup_{n \in \N} K_n =U\).

Note that \(L^q_{\mathrm{loc}}(U) \subset L^p_{\mathrm{loc} }(U)\) for all \(q \leq p\).

Theorem 1 (Locally integrable functions as distributions) The mapping

\[ \begin{aligned} T:L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(U) & \hookrightarrow \Dd'(U) \\ u & \longmapsto T_u . \end{aligned} \]

Defined by

\[ \begin{aligned} (\varphi,T_u):= \int_{U}\varphi u , \quad\forall \varphi\in C_c^\infty(U). \end{aligned} \tag{5}\]

Is injective and continuous.

Proof. We begin by showing that \(T_u \in \Dd '(U)\). Firstly, the integral in (5) is finite as \(\varphi\) has compact support. Secondly, if we write \(K\) for the support of \(\varphi\), it holds that

\[ \begin{aligned} (f,\varphi u)= \int_{K}\varphi f u \leq \norm{u}_{L^1(K)} \norm{\varphi f}_{C^0_c(K)} . \end{aligned} \]

Which, by our equivalent characterization of \(\Dd'(U)\) in (4) shows that \(T_u \in \Dd'(U)\). We now show that the identification is injective. That is, if

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{U}\varphi u=0 , \quad\forall \varphi \in C_c^\infty(U) . \end{aligned} \]

Then \(u=0\). Consider a compact set \(K \subset U\) and write \(g\) for the extension of \(\mathrm{sign}(u)\) by zero outside of \(K\). Clearly \(g \subset L^1(\R^d)\). Consider an approximation to the identity \(\phi_n\) (see Appendix Section 12 ) and set

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi_n := g*\phi_n. \end{aligned} \]

By the approximation and smoothing of Propositions Proposition 89 we obtain a bounded sequence with \(\varphi_n \subset C_c^\infty(U)\) for \(n\) large enough and such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{n \to \infty}\varphi_n =g \in L^1(\R^d) ; \quad \norm{\varphi_n}_{L^\infty(\R^d)} \leq \norm{g}_{L^\infty(\R^d)}=1. \end{aligned} \]

By the first part of the above, we may take a subsequence \(\varphi_{n_k}\) converging to \(g\) almost everywhere, and by the second we may apply the dominated convergence theorem to obtain that

\[ \begin{aligned} 0= \lim_{k \to \infty}\int_{U}\varphi_{n_k} u=\int_{K}\abs{u}. \end{aligned} \]

As a result, \(u=0\) vanishes on \(K\). Since \(K\) was any compact subset of \(U\) and every open set can be written as a union of compact sets we conclude that \(u=0\) as desired. The continuity of \(T\) follows from the estimate

\[ \begin{aligned} (\varphi,T_{u} -T_v)\leq \norm{\varphi}_{C_c^k(K)}\norm{u-v}_{L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)}. \end{aligned} \]

Where \(\varphi \in C_c^\infty(U)\) has support \(K\) (remember we are considering the weak-\(\star\) topology on \(\Dd'(U)\)).

Due to the above immersion, we will naturally consider \(L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) as a subspace of \(\Dd'(U)\). In particular, any subspaces of \(L^1_{\mathrm{loc} }(U)\) such as \(L^p(U)\) or \(C^\infty(U)\) can also be considered as distributions.

5.3 Support of a distribution

In the continuous case, the support of a function is well-defined as the smallest closed set outside of which the function is zero. However, when working with an equivalence class of functions the definition must be amended (consider for example the support of \(0=1_{\Q}\)). This is resolved by the following definitions.

Definition 6 We say that a distribution \(w \in \Dd'(U)\) vanishes on \(V \subset U\) if

\[ \begin{aligned} (\varphi, w)=0 , \quad\forall \varphi \in C_c^\infty(U) \text{ with } \supp{\varphi} \subset V . \end{aligned} \]

And write

\[ \begin{aligned} w =0 \text{ on } V. \end{aligned} \]

If a function vanishes on a collection of sets it also vanishes on their union, this extends to distributions.

Lemma 1 Let \(\set{U_\alpha}_{\alpha \subset I}\) be a collection of open sets in \(U\) and suppose that

\[ \begin{aligned} w=0 \text{ on } U_\alpha , \quad\forall \alpha \in I. \end{aligned} \]

Then \(w\) vanishes on \(U:=\bigcup_{\alpha \in I}U_\alpha\).

Proof. Let \(\varphi\) have support in \(U\). Then, by compactness, we can extract a finite covering \(\set{U_i}_{i=1}^n\) of \(\supp{\varphi}\). Let \(\set{\rho _i}_{i=1}^n\) be a partition of unity subordinate to \(U_i\) (see Appendix Section 13). Then

\[ \begin{aligned} (\varphi,\omega)=\qt{\sum_{i=1}^n \rho _i \varphi,w }=\sum_{i=1}^n (\rho _i \varphi,w )=0 . \end{aligned} \]

Since \(\varphi\) was any test function supported in \(U\) this concludes the proof.

By the just proved Lemma 1 we see that there is a largest set on which \(w\) vanishes. As a result, we can make the following definition.

Definition 7 Let \(w \in C_c^\infty(U)\) and let \(V\) be the largest open set on which \(w\) vanishes. Then, we define the support of \(w\) as

\[ \begin{aligned} \supp{w}= V^c . \end{aligned} \]

Since \(L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) is naturally included in \(\Dd'(U)\) we obtain in particular the definition of support of a function \(f \in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\).

Exercise 13 If \(f \in L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) the support of \(f\) is the complementary of the largest open set on which \(f\) is \(0\) almost everywhere. In particular, if \(f\) is continuous, the (distributional) support of \(f\) coincides with the classical support of \(f\).

TipHint

We saw in Theorem 1 that \(f\) is \(0\) almost everywhere on some open set if and only if it integrates to \(0\) against any test function on the open set. This shows the first part and the second follows immediately.

6 Sobolev spaces

Now that we have built the space of distributions we can define weak derivatives of test functions just as we did with tempered distributions.

Definition 8 Given \(w \in \Dd'(U)\) we define the (weak) \(\alpha\)-th derivative by

\[ \begin{aligned} (\varphi,D^\alpha w):=(-1)^{\abs{\alpha} }(D^\alpha \varphi, w), \quad \forall \varphi \in C_c^\infty(U). \end{aligned} \]

TipHint

A prerequisite for the definition to make sense is that the notion corresponds to that of classical derivative.

Exercise 15 Let \(u \in C_{\mathrm{loc}}^1(U)\) have classical derivatives \(u^{(i)}\). Then \(u^{(i)}= \partial_i u\).

TipHint

By definition of weak derivatives and the chain rule, we have the distributional equality

\[ \begin{aligned} T_{u^{(i)}}-T_{\partial _i u}=T_{u^{(i)}-\partial _i u}=0 \in \Dd'(U) . \end{aligned} \]

The result follows by the injectivity of \(T\).

Definition 9 (Sobolev spaces) Given an open set \(U \subset \R^d\), \(k \in \N\) and \(p \in [1,\infty]\) we define the Sobolev space

\[ \begin{aligned} W^{k,p}(U):=\set{ u: D^{\alpha}u \in L^p(U) \hookrightarrow \Dd'(U), \quad\forall \abs{\alpha}\leq k } . \end{aligned} \]

Where \(L^p(U)\) is identified as a subspace of \(\Dd'(U)\) by Theorem 1. We give \(W^{k,p}(U)\) the norm

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{W^{k,p}(U)}&:=\sum_{\abs{\alpha}\leq k } \norm{D^\alpha u}_{L^p(U)}. \end{aligned} \]

That is, \(W^{k,p}(U)\) is the space of \(k\)-times (weakly) differentiable functions with derivatives in \(L^p(U)\). An equivalent norm that is also sometimes used is

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{W^{k,p}(U)}\sim \sum_{j=0}^k \norm{\nabla^j u}_{L^p(U\to \R^{d^j})} . \end{aligned} \]

The local Sobolev spaces \(W^{k,p}_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) are defined similarly, where we now only require that

\[ \begin{aligned} D^\alpha u \in L^p_{\text{loc}}(U) , \quad\forall \abs{\alpha}\leq k . \end{aligned} \]

And now generate the topology by local seminorms analogously to \(L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\).

Theorem 2 (Completeness of Sobolev spaces) For all \(k \in \N\) and \(p \in [1,\infty]\) both \(W^{k,p}(U)\) and \(W^{k,p}_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) are Banach spaces. If \(p \in (1,\infty)\) these spaces are also reflexive.

Proof. Let \(\set{u_n}_{n=1}^\infty\) be a Cauchy sequence in \(W^{k,p}(U)\). Then, by definition of the norm on \(W^{k,p}(U)\) the sequence of derivatives \(D^\alpha u_n\) is Cauchy in \(L^p(U)\) for each \(\abs{\alpha}\leq k\) and since \(L^p(U)\) is complete, converge to some functions \(u^{\alpha}\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{n \to \infty} D^\alpha u_n = u^{(\alpha)} \in L^p(U) , \quad\forall \abs{\alpha} \leq k . \end{aligned} \]

To conclude, it suffices to show that \(u:= u^{(0)}\) verifies

\[ \begin{aligned} D^\alpha u= u^{(\alpha)}. \end{aligned} \]

This holds as, for every test function \(\varphi \in \Dd(U)\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{U} u^{(\alpha)}\varphi =\lim_{n \to \infty}\int_{U} u_n^{(\alpha)}\varphi=\lim_{n \to \infty}(-1)^{\abs{\alpha} }\int_{U} u_nD^\alpha\varphi=(-1)^{\abs{\alpha} }\int_{U} uD^\alpha\varphi . \end{aligned} \]

Where the first and last inequality follows from the continuous immersion of \(L^p(U)\) in \(\Dd'(U)\) (Hölder’s inequality). Reflexivity follows from the fact that the mapping

\[ \begin{aligned} T: W^{k,p}(U) & \longrightarrow (L^p(U))_{\abs{\alpha}\leq k } \\ u & \longmapsto (D^{\alpha}(u))_{\abs{\alpha}\leq k } . \end{aligned} \]

Is an isometry, so \(\Im(T)\) is closed in the reflexive Banach space \((L^p(U))_{\abs{\alpha}\leq k }\) and thus reflexive (see (Brezis and Brézis 2011) page 70). The case of \(W^{k,p}_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) is proved identically now working with the local seminorms.

We now show some relevant properties of the weak derivative all of which are to be expected knowing the classical case. These can be greatly generalized with tools we will later develop.

Proposition 1 (Properties) Let \(u \in W^{k,p}(U)\). Then it holds that

  1. Leibniz rule: given \(\varphi \in C_c^1(U)\) it holds that

    \[ \begin{aligned} \partial _{i} (u \varphi)= \partial_i u \varphi+ u \partial _i \varphi . \end{aligned} \]

  2. For \(U = \R^d\), the translation \(\tau_y u(x):=u(x-y) \in W^{k,p}(\R^d)\) with \(D^\alpha \tau_y u = \tau_y D^\alpha u\).

  3. Let \(u \in W^{k,p}(\R^d)\) and \(v \in L^1(\R^d)\). Then

    \[ \begin{aligned} D^\alpha (v*u)= v* D^\alpha u \end{aligned} \]

Proof. Points 1 and 2 follow by the definition of weak derivative and the relevant properties for classical derivatives. The third property follows from the second as, by Fubini,

\[ \begin{aligned} & (v*u,D^\alpha\varphi ) = \int_{\R^d}v(y)\qt{\int_{\R^d}D^\alpha \varphi(x) u(x-y) \d x } \d y \\& =(-1)^{\abs{\alpha} } \int_{\R^d}v(y) \qt{\int_{\R^d}\varphi(x) D^\alpha u(x-y) \d x } \d y =(-1)^{\abs{\alpha} }(v*D^\alpha u, \varphi). \end{aligned} \]

A natural question is what relationship there is between Sobolev functions and classical derivatives. For example, in \(1\) dimension a classical result is that \(u \in W^{1,1}(a,b)\) if and only if \(u\) is absolutely continuous and has derivative almost everywhere. A more general result is as follows.

Proposition 2 (Absolute continuity on lines) The following are equivalent

  • \(u \in W^{1,p}_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\).

  • There exists a representative of \(u\) in \(L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) that is almost everywhere differentiable with classical derivatives \(u^{(i)} \in L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) and, given \(V \Subset U\) it holds that this representative is absolutely continuous on almost all (with respect to the Lebesgue measure on \(\R^{d-1}\)) line segments in \(V\) parallel to the coordinate axis.

The above holds true if we replace \(W^{1,p}_{\mathrm{loc}}(U),L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) with their none local counterparts \(W^{1,p}(U),L^p(U)\).

We omit the proof which can be found in (Kinnunen 2023) pages \(39-43\). The next exercise show that, perhaps somewhat unexpectedly, to have \(u \in W^{1,p}(U)\) it is not sufficient to require that \(u\) is differentiable almost everywhere with integrable derivatives.

Example 2 The devil’s staircase \(c\) is differentiable almost everywhere with \(c'=0\).

As a result, if \(c \in W^{1,p}(0,1)\) then \(c\) would be constant (which it is not). In fact, \(c \not\in W^{1,p}(0,1)\) as it is not absolutely continuous.

7 Smooth approximation of Sobolev functions

The definition of weak derivative requires one to integrate against smooth functions whenever trying to prove some property holds. This is somewhat cumbersome. One would much rather

  1. Work pretending all Sobolev functions are (classically) smooth.

  2. Manipulate them according to the standard rules of calculus.

  3. Obtain a result that holds for all Sobolev functions (and not just the classically smooth ones).

This process can be rigorously justified by the density of various spaces of smooth functions in Sobolev spaces. Or to use Tao’s terminology, by giving ourselves an epsilon of room.

In this section, we prove the relevant results. These rely heavily on the analogous density results for functions in \(L^p(U)\) (see Appendix Section 12). As a result, they will not when \(p=\infty\). We start without making any assumptions on \(U\) and obtain two local-type results. Throughout this section we will often be switching between different open sets and, if following the proofs, making some drawings is recommended.

Theorem 3 (Local approximation by smooth functions) Let \(u \in W^{k,p}(U)\) for \(p < \infty\), denote its extension by zero \(\tilde{u}\) and let \(\set{\phi_n}_{n=1}^\infty\) be an approximation to unity. Then

\[ \begin{aligned} u=\lim_{n \to \infty} \tilde{u}*\phi_n \in W^{k,p}(V) , \quad\forall V \Subset U. \end{aligned} \]

As a result, for any \(V \Subset U\),

\[ \begin{aligned} \overline{\restr{C_c^\infty(\R^d)}{V}}=\restr{W^{k,p}(U)}{V}\quad \text{ and } \quad \overline{\restr{C_c^\infty(\R^d)}{U}}=W_{\mathrm{loc}}^{k,p}(U) \end{aligned} \]

Proof. Given a compactly embedded set \(V \Subset U\) we can take \(n\) large enough so that \(V+ B \qt{0, \frac{1}{n}} \subset U\) and as a result, the convolution \(\varphi_n:= \tilde{u} * \phi_n\) verifies (see Observation Remark 6)

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi_n= u* \phi_n \quad \text{ on } V . \end{aligned} \]

Thus, by the third property Proposition 1, for all \(\abs{\alpha} \leq k\)

\[ \begin{aligned} D^\alpha \varphi_n = D^\alpha u * \phi_n \quad \text{ on } V. \end{aligned} \]

Taking limits we conclude from Proposition 8 that

\[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{n \to \infty}D^\alpha \varphi_n =D^\alpha u \in L^p(V) . \end{aligned} \]

To conclude density of \(C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) in \(\restr{W^{k,p}(U)}{V}\) consider \(K\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} V \subset K; \quad K+ B_{1 /n}\subset U. \end{aligned} \]

And a bump function \(\eta_V \in C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) that is equal to \(1\) on \(V\) and is supported in \(K\) (this is possible by Uryshon’s lemma and a convolution). Then,

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi_{V,n}:= \varphi_n \eta _V \in C_c^\infty(\R^d); \quad \lim_{n \to \infty}\varphi_{V,n}=u \in W^{k,p}(V). \end{aligned} \]

The density of \(C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) in \(W^{k,p}_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) follows by taking \(V_n\) converging to \(U\) and \(u_n:= \varphi_{V_n,n}\) as then, for every compactly included \(W \Subset U\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{n \to \infty}u_{n}=u \in W^{k,p}(W) . \end{aligned} \]

This concludes the proof as by Exercise 3 local convergence is equivalent to convergence on every compactly included subset.

Remark 3. Note that, without further assumptions on \(U\) it is impossible to get a global approximation by smooth functions defined on all of \(\R^d\). This is because, the convolution \(f* \phi_n\) can only be defined on

\[ \begin{aligned} U_{1/n}:= \set{x \in U: d(x,\partial U)> \frac{1}{n}} . \end{aligned} \]

The above issue disappears when \(U =\R^d\). This shows that

Theorem 4 (Global approximation in \(\R^d\)) It holds that for all \(p<\infty\),

\[ \begin{aligned} \overline{C_c^\infty(\R^d)}=W^{k,p}(\R^d). \end{aligned} \]

Proof. Let \(\eta \in C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) be equal to \(1\) on \(B_1\) and set \(\eta _n(x):=\eta (x /n)\). Then, given \(u \in W^{k,p}(\R^n)\) and a smooth approximation to unity \(\phi_n\) we obtain that, by the triangle inequality

\[ \begin{aligned} u= \lim_{n \to \infty}(u*\phi_n)\eta _n \in W^{k,p}(\R^d). \end{aligned} \]

The following Theorem shows a global-type approximation.

Theorem 5 (Meyers-Serrin: local till boundary approximation) Let \(U \subset \R^d\) be open, then for all \(p<\infty\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \overline{C^\infty_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\cap W^{k,p}(U)}=W^{k,p}(U). \end{aligned} \]

Proof. The proof is an instructive way of using a partition of unity to piece together a local result (in this case Theorem 3) to get a global one. Let \(\eps >0\) and consider an open covering \(\set{V_i}_{i=0}^\infty\) of \(U\) with \(V_0 =\emptyset\) and \(V_{i} \Subset V_{i+1}\). By Theorem 17 we may obtain a partition of unity \(\rho _i\) subordinate to the “rings” \(U_{i}:= V_{i+1}\setminus \overline{V_{i-1}}\) (the trickery with the indices is so that the \(U_i\) actually cover \(U\)). Where we relabel so that

\[ \begin{aligned} \supp{\rho _i}\subset U_i . \end{aligned} \]

We have that \(\rho_i u \in C_c^\infty({V_{i+1}})\) with \(V_{i+1} \Subset U\) so by the local approximation in Theorem 3 we can find \(n_i\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{\phi_{n_i}*(\rho_i u)-\rho_i u}_{W^{k,p}(U)}=\norm{\phi_{n_i}*(\rho_i u)-\rho_i u}_{V_{i+1}}\leq \frac{\eps }{2^i} . \end{aligned} \]

Now we obtain the global approximation by taking

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi:= \sum_{i=1}^\infty \phi_{n_i}* (\rho_i u) \in C_{\mathrm{loc}}^\infty(U) . \end{aligned} \]

As then

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{\varphi-u} _{W^{k,p}(U)}\leq\sum_{i=1}^\infty \norm{\phi_{n_i}*(\rho_i u)-\rho_i u }_{W^{k,p}(U)}\leq \sum_{i=1}^\infty \frac{\eps }{2^i}=\eps . \end{aligned} \]

Note that Theorem 5 is not strictly stronger than Theorem 3 as it is not, in general, possible to extend functions in \(C^\infty_{\mathrm{loc}}(U)\) to \(C_c^\infty(\R^n)\). As a corollary of Meyers-Serrin’s theorem, we obtain an equivalent definition of \(W^{k,p}(U)\).

Now we provide a final global approximation result in the case where the domain is smooth (see Appendix Section 14 for a review on manifolds with boundary) and bounded.

Theorem 6 (Global, smooth on boundary approximation) Let \(\Omega\) be a bounded open domain with \(C^1\) boundary, then for all \(p<\infty\).

\[ \begin{aligned} \overline{\restr{C_c^\infty(\R^d)}{\Omega}}= W^{k,p}(\Omega). \end{aligned} \]

Proof. The idea will be to locally translate points close to the boundary further into \(\Omega\) so that we can convolve with an approximation of unity and then recover the global case with a partition of unity. We begin by “straightening the boundary”. That is, since \(\partial \Omega\) is \(C^1\), given \(x_0 \in \partial \Omega\) there exists an open set \(V \subset \R^d\) and a function \(\gamma \in C^1(\R^{d-1})\) such that, relabeling and flipping the last coordinate axis if necessary

\[ \begin{aligned} V \cap \Omega = \set{x \in V :x_d >\gamma (x_1,\ldots,x_{d-1})} . \end{aligned} \]

By considering a translation in the last coordinate \(e_d=(0,\ldots,0,1)\) and its mollification by an approximation of unity \(\phi_n\)

\[ \begin{aligned} u_n(x):= u\qt{x+ \frac{2}{n}e_d }; \quad \varphi_n:= u_n *\phi_n . \end{aligned} \]

For \(n\) big enough we have that \(\varphi_n\) is well defined and smooth on \(W_0:=B_{\frac{1}{n}}(x_0)\). Since translation is continuous on \(L^p(U)\) for \(p \in [1,\infty)\) and by the behavior of differentiation with convolution (see Proposition 1), for \(n\) large enough

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u -\varphi_n}_{W^{k,p}(W_0)}\leq \norm{u -u_n}_{W^{k,p}(W_0)}+\norm{u_n -u_n*\phi_n}_{W^{k,p}(W_0)}\leq \eps. \end{aligned} \]

Now, since \(\Omega\) is bounded \(\partial \Omega\) is compact we may extract a finite covering \(\set{W_i:=B_{\frac{1}{n_i}}(x_i)}_{i=0}^n\) of \(\partial \Omega\) and functions \(\set{\varphi_i}_{i=1}^n\) smooth on \(W_i\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u -\varphi_n}_{W^{k,p}(W_i)}\leq \frac{\eps}{n+2} . \end{aligned} \]

Now we take an open set \(W_{n+1} \Subset \Omega\) such that \(\set{W_i}_{i=0}^{n+1}\) cover \(\Omega\). By the local approximation of Theorem 3 we know we can approximate \(u\) on \(W_{n+1}\) by some \(\varphi_{n+1} \in C_c^\infty(\R^d)\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u-\varphi_{n+1}}_{W^{k,p}(W_{n+1})}\leq\frac{\eps}{n+2} . \end{aligned} \]

Finally, we take a smooth partition of unity \(\set{\rho _i}_{i=0}^{n+1}\) subordinate to \(\set{W_i}_{i=0}^{n+1}\) and a bump function \(\eta \in C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) which is equal to \(1\) on \(\Omega\) and is supported on \(\bigcup_{i=0}^{n+1} W_i\) (see Example 5) and set

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi:= \eta \sum_{i=0}^{n+1} \rho _i \varphi_i \in C^\infty_c(\Omega) . \end{aligned} \]

This gives the desired approximation

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u-\varphi}_{W^{k,p}(\Omega)} \leq \sum_{i=0}^{n+1} \norm{\rho _i(u-\varphi_i)}_{W^{k,p}(W_i)} \leq \eps . \end{aligned} \]

This concludes the proof.

In contrast to Theorem 5, Theorem 6 shows that Sobolev functions on smooth bounded domains can be approximated by functions that are also smooth on the boundary of the domain \(\Omega\). This will prove fundamental in the next section. Both to extend them to the whole of \(\R^d\) and to restrict them to \(\partial \Omega\).

8 Extensions and restrictions

Many results in the theory of Sobolev spaces are proved first on \(\R^d\) and then extended to more general domains by a continuous extension followed by a restriction. For example, if one can show that \(W^{k,p}(\R^d) \in L^q(\R^d)\) for some \(p,q,r\) then, denoting by \(\tilde{u}\) the extension of \(u\) to \(\R^d\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{L^q(\Omega)} \sim \norm{u}_{L^q(\R^d)} \lesssim \norm{u}_{W^{k,p}(\R^d)} \sim \norm{u}_{W^{k,p}(\Omega)}, \end{aligned} \]

which shows that \(W^{k,p}(\R^d) \subset L^q(\Omega)\). In the reasoning above, it is necessary for the extension to be continuous, otherwise we fail on the first \(\sim\). As we saw previously, merely extending by zero will not do and in this section, we develop the necessary tools to extend Sobolev functions from a domain \(\Omega\) to \(\R^d\).

Definition 10 Let \(X(U)\) be a topological space of functions for each open \(U \subset \R^d\). We say that \(\Omega\) is an extension domain for \(X\) if there exists a continuous operator

\[ \begin{aligned} E: X(\Omega ) \to X(\R^d), \end{aligned} \]

such that \(Eu(x)=u(x)\) for almost all \(x \in \Omega\). We call \(E\) an extension operator.

For an extension to be possible, it is crucial that \(\Omega\) is regular enough. It will be sufficient to impose that \(\Omega\) have uniformly Lipschitz boundary. The following definition and Theorem can be found in (Leoni 2017, sec. 13).

Definition 11 We say that an open set \(\Omega \subset \R^d\) has uniformly Lipschitz boundary if there exists \(\eps,L>0, N \in \N\) and an open covering \(\set{U_n}_{n \in \N}\) of \(\partial \Omega\) such that

  1. For all \(x \in \partial \Omega\) there exists a \(U_n\) such that \(B_\eps(x) \subset U_n\).

  2. Every point in \(\R^d\) is contained in at most \(N\) sets \(U_n\).

  3. For each \(n\) there exist local coordinates \(y=(y', y_n) \in \R^{d-1} \times \R\) and a Lipschitz continuous function \(f: \R^{d-1} \to \R\) (both depending on \(n\)), with Lipschitz constant \(\operatorname{Lip} f \leq L\), such that \(U_n \cap \Omega=U_n \cap V_n\), where \(V_n\) is given in local coordinates by

    \[ \begin{aligned} \set{(x', x_d) \in \R^{d-1} \times \R: x_d>f(x')} . \end{aligned} \]

That is, a domain is uniformly Lipschitz if it has a locally finite covering which makes it of type \(C^{0,1}\) (see Appendix Section 14). Below we show two typical examples.

The following theorem shows that if \(\partial\Omega\) is uniformly Lipschitz, \(\Omega\) is an extension domain for \(W^{k,p}\). To simplify the proof we will give the proof in the case of bounded open sets with \(C^k\) boundary. Using the approximation of Sobolev functions by functions smooth on the boundary and a change of variables, we can extend functions in \(W^{k,p}(\Omega)\) to the whole of \(\R^d\).

Theorem 7 (General extension) Let \(\Omega\subset \R^d\) be an open set with uniformly Lipschitz boundary. Then \(\Omega\) is an extension domain for \(W^{k,p}(\Omega)\) and \(C^k(\Omega)\) for all \(p \in [1,\infty]\).

Proof. We prove the result when the domain is bounded with \(C^k\) boundary and when \(p<\infty\). The general result can be found in (Leoni 2017, 424).

We work first in the upper half-space (the canonical example of a manifold with boundary)

\[ \begin{aligned} \Hh^d =\set{x=(x_1,\ldots,x_d) \in \R^d : x_d \geq 0} . \end{aligned} \]

That is, we suppose that there is some open set \(\Omega' \subset \R^d\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \Omega=\Omega' \cap \Hh^d_{>0}. \end{aligned} \]

Where

\[ \begin{aligned} \quad \Hh^d_{>0} =\mathrm{int}(\Hh^d)=\set{x=(x_1,\ldots,x_d) \in \R^d : x_d >0} . \end{aligned} \]

By Theorem 6 we also give ourselves an epsilon of room by supposing that \(u \in C^k(\overline{\Omega})\). We define the extension of \(u\) to \(\Omega'\) as

\[ \begin{aligned} Eu(x) = \begin{cases} u(x) \quad & x_{d} \geq 0 \\ \sum_{j=1}^{k+1} a_j u_j(x - j e_d) \quad & x_d < 0 \end{cases}. \end{aligned} \]

We will have \(Eu \in C^k(\Omega')\) as long as we can get the derivatives to match up on the boundary \(\{x_d=0\}\cap \overline{\Omega'}\). That is, as long as \(a_j\) verify

\[ \begin{aligned} \sum_{j=1}^{k+1}(-j)^l a_j =1 , \quad l=0,1,\ldots,k . \end{aligned} \]

The above is a system of \(k+1\) equations with \(k+1\)-unknowns \(a_j\). Its matrix is the Vandermonde matrix (which is invertible). As a result, the system may be solved to extend \(u\). By the form of \(Eu\) we have the bound

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{Eu}_{W^{k,p}(\Omega')} \leq c\norm{u}_{W^{k,p}(\Omega)} . \end{aligned} \]

Where \(c:= (k+1)^{k+1}\max \set{1,\norm{a}_\infty}\). Working now in the general case for \(\Omega\), we may cover the compact \(\overline{\Omega}\) by a finite amount of bounded open sets \(\Omega_i \subset W\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi_i : \Omega_i \xrightarrow{\sim} \Omega_i'\quad \text{and} \quad \Phi_i : \Omega_i \cap \Omega \xrightarrow{\sim} \Omega_i' \cap \Hh^d . \end{aligned} \]

Where \(\Omega_i'\) are open in \(\R^{d}\) and \(\Phi_i\) are \(C^k\) diffeomorphisms. By the previous case, we can extend \(u'_i:=u \circ \Phi_i^{-1} \in C^k (\Omega_i' \cap \Hh^d)\) to functions \(\widetilde{u'_i} \in C^k(\Omega_i')\) and then transform back to the original space to get

\[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{u_i}:= \widetilde{u'_i} \circ \Phi_i \in C^k(\Omega_i). \end{aligned} \]

Where

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{\tilde{u_i}}_{W^{k,p}(\Omega_i)}\lesssim \norm{u_i}_{W^{k,p}(\Omega)}. \end{aligned} \tag{7}\]

The hidden constant depending only on \(c,\norm{\Phi_i}_{C^k(\Omega_i)}\norm{\Phi_i^{-1}}_{C^k(\Omega_i')}\). Next, using a partition of unity subordinate to \(\set{\Omega_i}_{i=1}^n\) we glue the local extensions together to form an extension to all of \(\Omega\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{u}:= \sum_{i=1}^n \rho _i{u_i} \in C^k(\Omega). \end{aligned} \]

The desired extension can now be obtained by multiplying \(\tilde{u}\) with a bump function \(\eta\) that is supported in \(W\) and equal to \(1\) on \(\Omega\).

\[ \begin{aligned} Eu := \eta \tilde{u} \in C^k(\R^d). \end{aligned} \]

By (7) we have that

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{E u}_{W^{k,p}(\R^d)} \lesssim \norm{u}_{W^{k,p}(\Omega)}. \end{aligned} \]

As a result, \(E\) is a (bounded) linear operator. So far we had considered \(u \in C^k(\overline{\Omega})\). Now, since by Theorem 6 the space \(C^k(\overline{\Omega})\) is dense in \(W^{k,p}(\Omega)\) we may extend \(E\) to a linear operator on the whole of \(W^{k,p}(\Omega)\). A verification shows that \(E\) is also bounded as an operator from \(C^k(\Omega)\) to \(C^k(\R^d)\). This concludes the proof.

9 Trace theorem

As we already discussed, a PDE often incorporates boundary information such as \(\restr{u}{\partial \Omega}=0\). This is well defined if \(u\) is continuous, however, if \(u \in W^{k,p}(U)\), and is thus only defined almost everywhere, then \(\restr{u}{\partial U}\) is a priori not well defined. The following theorem remedies this issue.

Proposition 3 (Trace) Let \(\Omega \subset \R^d\) be open with Lipschitz boundary and let \(p\in[1,\infty)\). Then there exists a continuous linear operator

\[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{Tr}: W^{1,p}(\Omega)\to L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(\partial \Omega) . \end{aligned} \]

Such that \(\operatorname{Tr}(u) =\restr{u}{\partial \Omega}\) for all \(u \in C(\overline{\Omega}) \cap W^{1,p}(\Omega)\).

Proof. We will only prove the case where \(\Omega\) is bounded and of class \(C^1\). The proof of the general statement can once more be found in (Leoni 2017, 592).

As in previous results, the trick is to suppose first \(u\) is smooth, work locally, and then obtain a global result using a partition of unity and the density in Theorem 6. \

Given \(x_0 \in \partial \Omega\) we take a open set \(U \subset \R^d\) containing \(x_0\). Flattening out the boundary by \(\Phi : U \simeq U'\) where necessarily the boundary is preserved

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi: U \cap \partial \Omega\iso U' \cap \partial \Hh^d, \end{aligned} \]

and using the extension ?@thm-extension to extend \(u':= u\circ \Phi\) to \(\widetilde{u}'\) with compact support \(K \subset \R^d\) we obtain by the <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem#:~:text=space

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{U'\cap \partial \Hh^d}\abs{u'}^p \leq\int_{\partial \Hh^d} \abs{\widetilde{u}'}^p=\int_{\Hh^d} \partial_{d} \abs{\widetilde{u}'}^p\leq\int_{\Hh^d} p\abs{\widetilde{u}'}^{p-1}\abs{\partial_{d} u} \lesssim \norm{\widetilde{u}'}_{W^{1,p}(\Hh^d)}^p . \end{aligned} \]

Where in the second inequality we used the chain rule and in the last Hölder’s inequality. Since \(\Phi^{-1}\) is \(C^k\) and by the continuity of the extension we obtain what we are looking for in

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{L^p(U \cap \partial \Hh^d)}\lesssim \norm{\widetilde{u}'}_{W^{1,p}(\Hh^d)}\lesssim \norm{u'}_{W^{1,p}(U'\cap \Hh^d)}\lesssim \norm{u}_{W^{1,p}(U\cap \Omega)} \end{aligned} \tag{8}\]

We had supposed \(u\) smooth, now taking a finite covering \(\set{U_i}_{i=1}^n\) of \(\partial \Omega\) (this is possible by compactness of \(\partial \Omega\)) and taking a subordinate partition of unity \(\rho _i\) we conclude from (8) that

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{\partial \Omega} \abs{u}^p =\sum_{i=1}^n \int_{ U_i \cap \partial \Omega} \abs{u}^p \lesssim \sum_{i=1}^n \norm{u}_{W^{1,p}(U_i \cap \Omega)}^p =\norm{u}_{W^{1,p}(U)}^p . \end{aligned} \]

That is,

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{\operatorname{Tr}(u)}_{L^p( \partial \Omega)}\lesssim \norm{u}_{W^{1,p}(U)} . \end{aligned} \]

Using Theorem 6 to extend \(\operatorname{Tr}\) continuously to \(W^{k,p}(\Omega)= \overline{\restr{C_c^\infty(\R^d)}{\Omega}}\) concludes the proof.

Note that, if \(\Omega\) is bounded, then \(L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(\partial \Omega)=L^p(\partial \Omega)\) so \(\operatorname{Tr}(u) \in L^p(\partial \Omega)\).

Definition 13 We define the trace of \(u \in W^{k,p}(\Omega)\) as \(\operatorname{Tr}(u)\). We also use the notation

\[ \begin{aligned} \restr{u}{\partial \Omega}:= \operatorname{Tr}(u). \end{aligned} \]

To get an estimate on the trace we paid \(1\)-degree of regularity. We can do better and only pay \({1}/{p}\) degrees of regularity. This uses the theory of <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_operator#For_p_=_1:~:text=

Theorem 8 For all real \(s> 1 /2\) the trace operator is a continuous operator

\[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{Tr}: H^s(\Omega) \to H^{s -\frac{1}{2}}(\Omega). \end{aligned} \]

Proof. Straightening out the boundary and using the extension operator as in the trace Proposition 3, it is sufficient to work in the case where \(u\) is smooth and defined on \(\Hh^{d}\). By Fourier inversion, if we write \(\xi=(\xi',\xi _d)\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \wh{\operatorname{Tr}(u)}(\xi ')=\int_{\R}\wh{u}(\xi) \d \xi _d . \end{aligned} \]

So, by Cauchy-Schwarz

\[ \begin{aligned} \abs{\wh{\operatorname{Tr}(u)}(\xi ')}^2\leq \int_{\R}\abs{\wh{u}(\xi)}^2\br{\xi }^{2s} \d \xi _d \int_{\R}\br{\xi }^{-2s} \d \xi _d. \end{aligned} \tag{9}\]

The change of variables \(\xi _d \to \br{\xi '}\xi _d\) shows that

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{\R}\br{\xi }^{-2s} \d \xi _d=\br{\xi' }^{-2(s-\frac{1}{2})} \int_{\R}\br{\xi _d}^{-2s}\d \xi _d\lesssim \br{\xi' }^{-2(s-\frac{1}{2})} \end{aligned} \tag{10}\]

Where in the inequality it was used that \(s> 1 /2\). We deduce from (9) and (10) on taking norms that

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{H^{s-1 /2}(\R^{d-1})} \lesssim \norm{u}_{H^s(\R^d)}. \end{aligned} \]

Which concludes the proof.

10 Sobolev embeddings and inequalities

Sobolev inequalities are relationships that bound the norm of \(u\) in different function spaces depending on how differentiable and integrable \(u\) is. For example, such a relationship could look like

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{W^{ l,p^* }(\Omega)}\leq \norm{u}_{W^{k,p}(\Omega)}. \end{aligned} \tag{11}\]

By considering the rescaling \(u(\lambda x)\), performing a change of variables, and taking \(\lambda\) to \(\infty\) we see that for such a relationship to hold it is necessary that

\[ \begin{aligned} l- \frac{d}{p^*}=k-\frac{d}{p} . \end{aligned} \tag{12}\]

The case \(k=l+1\) gives rise to the following definition

Definition 15 The Sobolev conjugate of \(1\leq p<d\) is \(p^*\) defined by

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{p^*}= \frac{1}{p} -\frac{1}{d} . \end{aligned} \]

Note that \(p<p^*\). The idea behind inequalities such as (11) is to cash in some differentiability for some integrability. The main results used to do this are based on the fundamental theorem of calculus. First, we need the following lemma.

Lemma 2 (Loomis-Whitney inequality) Let \(d \geq 1\), let \(f_1, \ldots, f_d \in L^p\left(\mathbb{R}^{d-1}\right)\) for some \(p \in (0,\infty]\), and define

\[ \begin{aligned} F_d\left(x_1, \ldots, x_d\right):=\prod_{i=1}^d f_i\left(x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1}, x_{i+1}, \ldots, x_d\right). \end{aligned} \]

Then,

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{F_d}_{L^{p/(d-1)}}\leq\prod_{i=1}^d\left\|f_i\right\|_{L^p\left(\mathbb{R}^{d-1}\right)} . \end{aligned} \tag{13}\]

Proof. The case \(d=2\) is immediate by Fubini. The general case follows from induction on \(d\) . We write \((x_1,\ldots x_{d+1})=(x',x_{d+1})\)

\[ \begin{aligned} & \norm{F_{d+1}}_{L^{p /d}(\R^{d+1})} =\qt{\int_{\R}\qt{\int_{\R^d} F_d(x)^{\frac{p}{d}} f_{d+1}(x')^{\frac{p}{d}} \d x' } \d x_{d+1} }^{\frac{d}{p} } \\ & \leq \qt{\int_{\R} \qt{\int_{\R^d} F_d(x)^{\frac{p}{d-1}} \d x' }^{\frac{d-1}{d}}\d x_{d+1} }^{\frac{d}{p} }\norm{f_{d+1}}_{L^p(\R^d)} \notag \\ & \leq \qt{\int_{\R} \prod_{i=1}^d\left\|f_i(x_{d+1})\right\|_{L^p\left(\mathbb{R}^{d}\right)}^{\frac{p}{d}} \d x_{d+1}}^{\frac{d}{p}}\norm{f_{d+1}}_{L^p(\R^{d})} \end{aligned} \tag{14}\]

Where in the first inequality we applied Cauchy-Schwarz with \(q= d /(d-1), q' =d\). Now applying the general version of Hölder’s inequality \[ \begin{aligned} \norm{g_1\cdots g_n }_{L^1} \leq \norm{g_1}_{L^{p_1}}\cdots\norm{g_1}_{L^{p_n}}; \quad \frac{1}{p_1}+\cdots \frac{1}{p_n}=1\notag . \end{aligned} \]

to \(g_i:= \norm{f_i(\cdot )}^{\frac{p}{d}}_{L^p(\R^{d})} \in L^{d}(\R)\) gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{\R}\prod_{i=1}^{d}g_i \leq \prod_{i=1}^{d} \norm{g_i}_{L^d(\R)} . \end{aligned} \]

Substituting into (14) concludes the proof.

Using the Loomis-Whitney inequality and the fundamental theorem of calculus gives us our first Sobolev inequality

Theorem 9 (Sobolev-Gagliardo-Niremberg) Given \(1 \leq p<d\) it holds that

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{L^{p^*}(\R^d)}\lesssim \norm{\nabla u}_{L^p (\R^d)}. \end{aligned} \]

Proof. By density (see Theorem 4), it is enough to take \(u \in C_c^\infty(\R^d)\). Applying the fundamental theorem of calculus gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \abs{u}^m=\int_{-\infty}^\cdot \partial _i \abs{u}^m \d x_i \leq m\int_{\R}\abs{u}^{m-1}\abs{\partial _i u} \d x_i=: f_i , \quad\forall i=1,\ldots,d. \end{aligned} \]

Multiplying all these inequalities together gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \abs{u}^{md} \leq \prod_{i=1}^{d} f_i . \end{aligned} \]

Applying the Loomis-Whitney inequality (13) “with \(p=1\)” and Hölder’s inequality shows

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{L^{ md /(d-1)}(\R^d)}^{md/(d-1)}\lesssim \prod_{i=1}^{d} \norm{u^{m-1}\partial_iu}_{L^1(\R^d)} \leq \norm{u}_{L^{(m-1)p'}(\R^d)}^{(m-1)d}\norm{\nabla u}_{L^{p}(\R^d \to \R^d)}^d \end{aligned} \tag{15}\]

It remains to choose \(m\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \frac{md}{d-1}= (m-1)p' \implies m= \frac{(d-1)p^*}{d} =\frac{dp -p}{d-p} \geq 1. \end{aligned} \]

Substituting into (15) gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{L^{ p^*}(\R^d)}^{m}\lesssim \norm{u}_{L^{p^*}(\R^d)}^{m-1}\norm{\nabla u}_{L^{p}(\R^d \to \R^d)} . \end{aligned} \]

This concludes the proof.

Proof. Let \(u_n \in W^{k+l,p}(U)\) be a bounded sequence. By assumption \(W^{k,p}(\Omega)\) is compact in \(L^q(\Omega)\), so we may extract a subsequence \(u_{n_k}\) such that, for \(\abs{\alpha} \leq l\),

\[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{k \to \infty}D^\alpha u_{n_k} = u^{(\alpha)} \in L^q(\Omega) . \end{aligned} \]

Using the same technique as in Theorem 2 shows that \(u^{(\alpha)}= D^{\alpha}u\). This shows that \(u_{n_k} \to u\) in \(W^{l,q}(\Omega)\) and, by the equivalence of compactness and sequential compactness, concludes the proof.

This result can be combined directly with Rellich-Kondrachov Theorem 12 or any other embedding. For example, when combined with Corollary 1 we get

\[ \begin{aligned} W^{k+l,p}(\Omega) \hookrightarrow W^{l,q}(\Omega). \end{aligned} \]

In particular, for all \(p \in [1, \infty]\), the following inclusion is compact

\[ \begin{aligned} W^{k+l,p}(\Omega) \hookrightarrow W^{l,p}(\Omega). \end{aligned} \]

Proof. The proof follows in an identical method as the aforementioned results as we still have density of smooth functions \(W^{k,p}_0(U)\) and the extension theorem, which is now given by Exercise 22.

The main utility of all these compact embeddings is that given a sequence \(u_n\) whose derivatives are bounded in certain \(L^p\) norms we can extract convergent subsequences in appropriate spaces. We end this post (modulo appendices) with one of the most useful inequalities which we will make use of in future posts.

Theorem 14 (Poincaré inequality) Let \(u \in W^{1,p}_0(U)\) where \(U\) is bounded in one direction. Then

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{L^p(U)}\leq C\norm{\nabla u}_{L^p(U \to \R^d)}. \end{aligned} \]

Proof. By density (which holds by definition of \(W^{k,p}_0(U)\)) of it is sufficient to reason for \(u \in C_c^\infty(U)\) and pass to the limit. By relabeling we may suppose \(U\) is bounded along the \(x_d\) axis. That is, for some finite \(a<b\)

\[ \begin{aligned} U \subset \R^{d-1} \times [a,b]. \end{aligned} \]

The idea is to use the fundamental theorem of calculus together with the compact support of \(u\). This allows us to rewrite

\[ \begin{aligned} \abs{u(x)}= \abs{\int_{a}^{x_d} \partial_d u(x',t) \d t} \leq \qt{\int_{\R}\abs{\partial _d u(x',t)}^p \d t}^\frac{1}{p}(x_d-a)^{\frac{1}{p'}} . \end{aligned} \]

Taking \(L^p(\R^d)\) norms above gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{u}_{L^p(\R^d)}\leq \norm{\partial _du}_{L^p(\R^d)}\frac{p'}{p+p'}(b-a)^{\frac{p+p'}{p'}} \lesssim \norm{\nabla u}_{L^p(\R^d \to \R^d)} . \end{aligned} \]

This concludes the proof.

11 Convolutions and regularization

The convolution of two functions \(f,g\) can be thought of as “blurring” \(f\) by averaging it against \(g\). In the case where \(g\) is smooth, this blurring has the effect of smoothing out any sharp edges and irregularities in \(f\). This allows us to approximate irregular functions by smooth ones and serves as an important technical tool in our analysis.

Definition 17 (Convolution of functions) Given \(f\) and \(g\) we define the convolution of \(f,g\) to be the function \(f *g\)

\[ \begin{aligned} f *g := \int_{\R^d} f(y)g(x-y) \d y \end{aligned} \tag{18}\]

The definition given by (18) is purposefully vague. We still need to specify what spaces \(f,g\) belong to so that \(f*g\) is a well-defined element (of a further unspecified space). This can be done as follows.

Proposition 6 (Young’s convolution inequality) Consider the definition in (18) and let \(p,q,r \in [1, \infty]\). Then it holds that

  1. If \(f \in L^1(\R^d)\) and \(g \in L^p(\R^d)\) then \(f*g \in L^p(\R^d)\) with

    \[ \begin{aligned} \norm{f*g}_{L^p(\R^d)}\leq \norm{f}_{L^1(\R^d)}\norm{g}_{L^p(\R^d)}. \end{aligned} \]

  2. Suppose that

    \[ \begin{aligned} \frac{1}{p}+\frac{1}{q}=1+\frac{1}{r}; \quad f \in L^p(\R^d); \quad g \in L^q(\R^d) . \end{aligned} \]

    Then \(f*g \in L^r(\R^d)\) with

    \[ \begin{aligned} \norm{f*g}_{L^r(\R^d)}\leq \norm{f}_{L^p(\R^d)}\norm{g}_{L^q(\R^d)}. \end{aligned} \]

  3. In any of the above cases \(f*g=g*f\).

Proof. The first point follows from the triangle inequality for the Bochner integral (in this context this is also called Minkowski’s integral inequality) as

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{\int_{\R^d}f(y)g(\cdot -y) \d y}_{L^p(\R^d)}\leq\int_{\R^d}\norm{f(y)g(\cdot -y) \d y}_{L^p(\R^d)}= \norm{f}_{L^1(\R^d)} \norm{g}_{L^p(\R^d)}. \end{aligned} \]

To see the second point fix \(f\) and define the linear operator \(T_fg:= f*g\). Then, for \(g \in L^1(\R^d)\) and \(g \in L^{p'}(\R^d)\) respectively

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{T_f g}_{L^p(\R^d)} \leq \norm{f}_{L^p(\R^d)} \norm{g}_{L^1(\R^d)}; \quad \norm{T_f g}_{L^\infty(\R^d)} \leq \norm{f}_{L^p(\R^d)} \norm{g}_{L^{p'}(\R^d)}; . \end{aligned} \]

Where the first inequality is point one and the second follows from Cauchy-Schwarz. Now applying < https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz

The definition of convolution can be extended to even more settings, for example, suppose that \(g\) is the density of some finite (possibly signed) measure \(\mu\) and \(f\) is bounded, then

\[ \begin{aligned} f*\mu(x):= f*g(x)= \int_{\R^d}f(x-y) g(y)\d y = \int_{\R^d} f(x-y)\d \mu (y) . \end{aligned} \]

Definition 18 (Convolution of function with measure) Let \(\mu\) be a finite signed Borel measure on \(\R^d\) and \(f \in L^p(\R^d)\) then we define the convolution

\[ \begin{aligned} f*\mu (x) :=\int_{\R^d} f(x-y)\d \mu (y) \in L^p(\R^d). \end{aligned} \]

Note that, once more by the triangle inequality, the convolution is well-defined with

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{f* \mu }_{L^p(\R^d)} \leq \norm{f}_{L^p(\R^d)} \norm{\mu }_{TV} . \end{aligned} \]

Now, if we consider \(f,g\) to be the densities of some finite (signed) measures \(\mu,\nu\) then we obtain that for bounded \(h\)

\[ \begin{aligned} (h, \mu *\nu) & := \int_{\R^d} h(x) f*g(x) \d x = \int_{\R^d}\int_{\R^d} h(x+y) f(x) g(y)\d x \d y \\ & = \int_{\R^d \times\R^d} f(x+y) \d (\mu \otimes \nu)(x,y)\notag . \end{aligned} \tag{19}\]

That is, the convolution of \(\mu\) with \(\nu\) is the pushforward of the product measure \(\mu \otimes \nu\) with the sum \(S(x,y)\).

Definition 19 (Convolution of measures) Let \(\mu ,\nu\) be two finite signed measures on \(\Bb(\R^d)\). Then the convolution of \(\mu *\nu\) is the pushforward

\[ \begin{aligned} \mu *\nu := S\# (\mu \otimes\nu). \end{aligned} \]

The language of random variables can give some good motivation for this

Example 3 Let \(X,Y\) be random variables with law \(\mu ,\nu\) then \(X+Y\) has law \(\mu *\nu\). Furthermore, if \(X, Y\) are independent and \(\mu,\nu\) are absolutely continuous with densities \(f,g\) then \(X+Y\) is absolutely continuous with density \(f*g\).

Proof. The first part is by definition of pushforward. To show that \(\mu *\nu\) has density \(f*g\) it suffices to read the reasoning in equation (19) backward.

Through the random variable interpretation, we also see that if \(\mu,\nu\) have all their mass in \(A, B\) then their convolution must have all its mass in \(A+B\). That is,

Lemma 3 Let \(f,g,\mu,\nu\) be such that the convolution is well-defined. Then

\[ \begin{aligned} \supp{f*g}\subset \overline{\supp{f}+\supp{g}}; \quad \supp{\mu*\nu }=\overline{\supp{\mu }+\supp{\nu}}. \end{aligned} \]

Proof. This follows directly from the definition of convolution.

One technical point is that to define the convolution of two objects it is required that they be defined globally. For example if \(U \subsetneq \R^d\), we can’t convolve \(f \in L^p(\R^d)\) with \(g \in L_1(U)\) as the integral

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_{\R^d}f(y)g(x-y) \d y \end{aligned} \]

requires we evaluate \(f\) on all of \(\R^d\). One workaround is, if \(\phi\in L^1(\R^d)\) with \(\supp{\phi}\subset \overline{B(0,\eps ) }\) we can extend \(f\) to be equal to some \(g \in L^p(\R)\) outside of \(U\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{f}(x)=\begin{cases} f(x) \quad & x \in U \\ g(x) \quad & x \not\in U \end{cases}. \end{aligned} \]

Then, the convolution \(\tilde{f}*g\) is well defined and equal to

\[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{f}*\phi(x) = \int_{B(x,\eps )\cap U} f(y)\phi(x-y) \d y+\int_{B(x,\eps )\cap U^c} g(y)\phi(x-y) \d y . \end{aligned} \]

As we can see, the convolution in general depends on how we extend \(f\) outside of \(U\). However, it is independent of the extension for \(x\) in

\[ \begin{aligned} U_\eps :=\set{x \in U : d(x,\partial U)> \eps }. \end{aligned} \]

With

\[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{f}*\phi(x) = \int_{B(x,\eps )} f(y)\phi(x-y) \d y , \quad\forall x \in U_\eps . \end{aligned} \]

For this reason, we will employ the following notation.

Definition 20 Given \(f \in L^p(U)\) and \(\phi\in L^1(\R^d)\) with \(\supp{\phi}\subset \overline{B(0,\eps ) }\) we define \(f*\phi \in L^p(U_\eps )\) as

\[ \begin{aligned} f*\phi(x):= \int_{B(x,\eps )}f(y)\phi(x-y) \d y . \end{aligned} \]

\[ \begin{aligned} T*\varphi(\phi):=T(\tl{\varphi}*\phi) \quad \text{ where } \tl{\varphi}(x):=\varphi(-x) . \end{aligned} \]

In the above we can also swap all occurrences of \(C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) and \(\Dd'(\R^d)\) by \(\Ss(\R^d)\) and \(\Ss'(\R^d)\) respectively. An interesting fact is that the convolution of a distribution with a function is itself a function.

12 Smoothing in \(L^p\)

In this section, we examine how convolution can be used to approximate functions by smoother ones. This is of great practical use as it allows us to

  1. Consider an appropriate space of functions for our problem, smooth or otherwise.

  2. Perform formal manipulations using the standard rules of calculus as if all functions in this space were smooth and compactly supported until we obtain a desired result.

  3. Pass to the limit to recover the expression for the whole class of functions.

A crucial tool in this program is the following:

Definition 22 We say that a family of functions \(\{\phi_n\}_{n= 1}^\infty \subset L^1(\R^d)\) is an approximation to unity if

  • Norm 1: \(\norm{\phi_n}_{L^1(\R^d)}=1\).

  • Decreasing support: \(\supp{\phi_n} \subset B(0, 1 /n)\).

If \(\phi_n \in C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) we say that \(\phi_n\) are smooth.

Remark 5. The above definition is frequently also given letting the index set range over \(\eps \in \R_+\) and taking \(\supp{\phi_\eps } \subset B(0,\eps )\). This is equivalent and simply leads to taking \(\eps \to 0\) instead of \(n \to \infty\).

The first question is whether a smooth approximation to the identity exists. In the following example, we answer this in the affirmative.

Example 4 Let \(\varphi \in C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) then

\[ \begin{aligned} \phi_n:= \frac{n^d }{\norm{\varphi}_{L^1(\R^d)}}\varphi(nx) \end{aligned} \]

is a smooth approximation of the identity. Additionally,

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi(x):= \exp({\frac{1}{\abs{x}^2-1}}) 1_{B(0,1)} \in C_c^\infty(\R^d) \end{aligned} \tag{20}\]

By Proposition 6 \((L^1(\R^d),*)\) is a Banach algebra. However, it is a non-unital one. That is there does not exist an element \(e\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} f*e=f , \quad\forall f \in L^1(\R^d). \end{aligned} \]

However, we will soon see that in a limiting sense, an identity for the convolution exists. First, we need the following lemma.

Lemma 4 The space \(C_c(\R^d)\) is dense in \(L^p(\R^d)\) for all \(p \in [1,\infty)\).

Proof. Consider \(f \in L^p(\R^d)\). If \(f =1_A\) for some measurable set \(A\) with finite measure then, by the outer and inner regularity of the Lebesgue measure we may take \(U, K\) open and compact respectively with \(U \subset A \subset K\) and

\[ \begin{aligned} \lambda (K)-\lambda (A) <\eps . \end{aligned} \]

Where we wrote \(\mu\) for the Lebesgue measure on \(\R^d\). By <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urysohn

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{f- \varphi}_{L^p(\R^d)} \leq \eps . \end{aligned} \]

Since the space of simple functions is dense in \(L^p(\R^d)\) this concludes the proof.

The name “approximation of unity” in Definition Definition 22 is justified by the following proposition.

Proposition 8 Let \(f \in L^p(\R^d)\) where \(p \in [1,\infty)\) and consider \(g \in C_c(\R^d)\) and an approximation to unity \(\phi_n\). Then it holds that

\[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{n \to \infty}g*\phi_n=g \in C_c(\R^d);\quad \lim_{n \to \infty}f*\phi_n=f \in L^p(\R^d). \end{aligned} \]

Proof. Consider \(\eps >0\). Using that \(\phi_n\) has mass \(1\) and is supported on \(B(0,1/n)\).

\[ \begin{aligned} g*\phi_n(x)-g(x) & = \int_{B(0, \frac{1}{n})}(g(x-y)-g(x)) \phi_n(y) \d y . \end{aligned} \]

Now taking norms and \(n\) large enough gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{g*\phi_n-g}_{L^\infty(\R^d)} \leq \int_{B(0, \frac{1}{n})} \norm{g(\cdot -y) -g}_{L^\infty(\R^d)} \phi_n(y) \d y\leq \eps . \end{aligned} \tag{21}\]

Where in the last inequality we used that \(g\) is uniformly continuous and \(\phi_n\) has mass \(1\). Since \(\eps >0\) was any, this shows the first part of the proposition.

We now prove the second part. By Lemma 4 we can choose \(g\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{g-f}_{L^p(\R^d)}<\eps . \end{aligned} \]

Now, since \(K_n:=\supp{g* \phi_n} \subset K + B(0, 1 /n)\), whose measure is bounded by some \(M>0\), the inequality in (21) shows that

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{g*\phi_n-g}_{L^p(\R^d)}^p \leq \int_{K_n} \eps^p \d y\leq M\eps^p . \end{aligned} \]

Now using the triangle inequality and Young’s convolution inequality Proposition 6 gives

\[ \begin{aligned} \norm{f*\phi_n-f}_{L^p(\R^d)} & \leq\norm{(f-g)*\phi_n}_{L^p(\R^d)}+\norm{g*\phi_n-g}_{L^p(\R^d)} \\ & +\norm{g-f}_{L^p(\R^d)}\leq \eps + M^{\frac{1}{p}}\eps +\eps . \end{aligned} \]

This shows the second part and concludes the proof.

13 Global to local and back again

Often it is advantageous to work locally and then reason in the general case by some kind of approximation. A useful tool in this respect are bump functions.

Definition 23 A bump function (also called cutoff function) is a function \(\eta \in C_c^\infty(\R^n)\).

Constructing bump functions that have some desired support is a tool we use frequently throughout. Here we provide two examples to show how this may be done. Other constructions are of course possible.

Proof. Since \(V \Subset U\), there exists a compact \(K\) with

\[ \begin{aligned} V \subset K \subset U;\quad d_u:=d(U,K)>0; \quad d_V:=d(V,K)>0. \end{aligned} \]

Now, by <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urysohn

Example 6 There exists a sequence of \(\eta_n \in C_c^\infty(\R^d)\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \eta_n(x) =\begin{cases} 1 \text{ if } & \abs{x}\leq n \\ 0 \text{ if } & \abs{x}\geq 2n \\ \end{cases}. \end{aligned} \]

And \(\norm{\eta_n}_{C^k(\R^d)} \leq \norm{\eta_1}\).

In the opposite direction. One is often in the situation where it is possible to derive some local properties for a given object (think manifolds). To recover a global result one needs some way to piece together the local results. A useful tool in this respect is partitions of unity.

Definition 24 Given a manifold \(M\) and an open covering \(\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in J}\) of \(M\) we say that \(\{\rho_i\}_{i\in I}\) is a partition of unity on \(\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in J}\) if:

  1. \(\supp{\rho_i}\subset U_\alpha\) for some \(\alpha \in J\).

  2. For each \(x \in M\), it holds that \(x \in \supp{\rho_\alpha}\) for only a finite amount of \(\alpha \in I\).

  3. \(\sum_{i \in I}\rho _i =1.\)

Partitions of unity are often used in differential geometry as follows

  1. Work in some open subset \(\R^n\) (or the upper half space \(\R^n_+\) if our manifold has boundary) to prove the existence of some object \(g\) with desired properties.

  2. Cover the manifold \(M\) with coordinate charts \(U_\alpha\) and translate the euclidean result via the identification with \(U_\alpha\) to obtain locally defined \(g_\alpha\).

  3. Obtain a globally defined object \(g\) by using the partition of unity to piece together the local objects

    \[ \begin{aligned} g=\sum_{\alpha \in I}\rho_\alpha g_\alpha . \end{aligned} \]

In addition to the approximation and extension theorems in Section Section 8, partitions of unity can be used to show that: every manifold has a Riemannian metric, show that a function is smooth on some non-open set \(S \subset M\) iff it is the restriction of a smooth function defined on a neighborhood of \(S\), prove the existence of an outward pointing vector on manifolds with boundary, define integration over an orientable manifold \(M\), prove Stokes’ theorem.

Theorem 17 Let \(M\) be a smooth manifold (in particular we assume \(M\) is Hausdorff), then every open covering \(\set{U_\alpha}_{\alpha \in J}\) has a partition of unity \(\set{\rho _n}_{n=1}^\infty\).

The proof is based on the existence of bump functions (something we have already proved for \(\R^d\)) and is straightforward in the case that \(M\) is compact. The general case can be reduced to the compact setting by obtaining a covering by relatively compact open sets \(\set{U_i}_{i=0}^\infty\) of \(M\) such that \[ \begin{aligned} V_{i} \Subset V_{i+1}. \end{aligned} \]

And then working with the compact \(\overline{V_{i+1}} \setminus V_{i}\). See (Tu 2011) Appendix C for the details.

14 Manifolds with boundary

We will be defining differential equations on open domains \(\Omega \subset \R^n\). In this case \(\overline{\Omega}\) will be a “manifold with boundary” whose regularity will determine what results we have access to. The prototypical example of a manifold with a boundary is the upper half space

\[ \begin{aligned} \Hh^d:=\set{x=(x_1,\ldots,x_d)\in \R^d: x_d \geq 0} . \end{aligned} \]

Here the inequality is not strict so that the boundary of \(\Hh^d\) is included in itself. Since \(\Hh^d\) is not open we need to define what is meant by saying that a function is differentiable on such a set.

Definition 25 Let \(S \subset \R^d\) be an arbitrary set. We say that a function \(f:S \to \R\) is \(k\)-times differentiable at \(p\) if there exists a function \(\tl{f}:\R^d \to \R\) which is \(k\) times differentiable at \(p\) and such that \(\tl{f}=f\) on \(S\).

Definition 26 If \(f\) is \(k\)-times differentiable at every point of \(S\) we say that \(f \in C^k(S)\).

Exercise 30 Show that \(f\in C^k(S)\) if and only if there exists an extension \(\tl{f}\in C^k(\R^d)\) of \(f\) which is equal to \(f\) on \(S\).

TipHint

Use a partition of unity.

A manifold with boundary \(M\) is just a generalization of \(\Hh^d\), where we impose that \(M\) is “locally equal” to \(\Hh^d\). That is, there exists a covering of \(M\) by open sets \(V_\alpha\) and a collection of homeomorphisms with the subspace topology from \(\Hh^d\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi_\alpha : V_\alpha \iso \Phi(V_\alpha). \end{aligned} \tag{22}\]

And where for compatibility we impose that for each \(\alpha,\beta\) the functions

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi_\beta \circ\Phi_\alpha^{-1}: \Phi_\alpha(V_\alpha\cap V_\beta ) \longmapsto \Phi_\beta (V_\alpha\cap V_\beta ) \end{aligned} \tag{23}\]

are diffeomorphisms on \(\Hh^d\) (again with the subspace topology). Here \(\Aa=\set{(V_\alpha,\Phi_\alpha)}\) is an atlas of \(M\). We say that \(\Aa\) is \(C^k\) if the diffeomorphisms in (23) are \(C^k\) (see Definition Definition 25).

Definition 27 We say that \((M,\Aa)\) is a \(C^k\) manifold with boundary if \(M\) is a second countable Hausdorff space and \(\Aa\) is a \(C^k\) atlas.

The boundary of \(M\) is its points that are mapped to the boundary \(\set{x_d=0}\) in \(\Hh^d\).

Definition 28 Given a point \(p \in M\) we say that \(p\) is a boundary point if for some (and thus every chart) \(\Phi_\alpha(p) \in \partial \Hh ^{d}\). We call the set of all boundary points the boundary of \(M\) and denote it by \(\partial M\).

Exercise 31 Show that the restricted atlas \(\restr{\Phi_\alpha}{\partial M}\) makes \(\partial M\) a \(d-1\) dimensional manifold without boundary.

TipHint

By definition of boundary

\[ \begin{aligned} \restr{\Phi_\alpha}{\partial M}: V_\alpha \cap M \to \partial \Hh^{d-1} \simeq \R^{d-1}. \end{aligned} \tag{24}\]

And the coordinate changes are \(C^k\) as the restriction of a \(C^k\) map is \(C^k\).

In our case we will always take \(M\) to be a subset of \(\R^d\), in this case, different variations of the above definition are possible. For example, by the inverse function theorem and Exercise 30, we can extend the functions \(\Phi_\alpha\) to diffeomorphisms on \(U_\alpha\) open in \(\R^d\) so that (22)-(24) now read

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi_\alpha : U_\alpha \iso \Phi(U_\alpha); \quad \Phi_\alpha : U_\alpha \cap M \iso \Phi_\alpha(U_\alpha) \cap \Hh^d . \end{aligned} \tag{25}\]

Additionally, by the implicit function theorem there exists for each coordinate set \(U_\alpha\) functions \(\gamma_\alpha \in C^k(\R^{d-1})\) such that, relabeling the coordinates and decreasing the size of \(U_\alpha\) if necessary,

\[ \begin{aligned} \partial M \cap U_\alpha & =\set{x \in U_\alpha : x_d=\gamma_\alpha(x_1,\ldots,x_{d-1}) } \end{aligned} \tag{26}\]

Let us write \(x=(x',x_d)\), by the Taylor expansion

\[ \begin{aligned} \Phi_\alpha(x',\gamma _\alpha(x')+\eps)=\Phi_\alpha(x)+\frac{\partial \Phi}{\partial x_d}(x)\eps +O(\norm{\eps }^2) . \end{aligned} \]

We deduce that, once more reducing the size of \(U_\alpha\) if necessary and depending on the sign of \(\partial _d \Phi\) on \(U_\alpha\), one and only one of the following two hold

\[ \begin{aligned} (M \setminus \partial M) \cap U_\alpha & =\set{x \in U_\alpha : x_d>\gamma_\alpha(x_1,\ldots,x_{d-1}) } \\ (M \setminus \partial M) \cap U_\alpha & =\set{x \in U_\alpha : x_d<\gamma_\alpha(x_1,\ldots,x_{d-1}) }\notag. \end{aligned} \tag{27}\]

The equivalent formulations in (25) and in (26)-(27) are used in the main exposition. Finally, in our case, we will typically take \(M =\overline{\Omega}\) where \(\Omega \subset \R^d\) is some open set. In this case, we adopt the following terminology.

Definition 29 We say that an open set \(\Omega\subset \R^d\) has \(C^k\) boundary of \(\overline{\Omega}\) is a \(C^k\) manifold with boundary.

In the above case, the topological and manifold boundaries of \(\Omega\) necessarily coincide as homeomorphisms map topological boundaries to topological boundaries.
