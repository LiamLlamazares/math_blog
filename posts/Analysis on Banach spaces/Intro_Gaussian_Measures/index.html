2 One dimensional Gaussians

The theory of Gaussian measures is built by considering first the \(\R\) valued case

Definition 1 We say that \(\mu\) is a Gaussian measure on \(\Bb(\R)\) if there exist \(m \in \R, \sigma>0\) such that either \(\mu=\delta_m\) or \(\mu\) has density

\[ \begin{aligned} p(t)= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2} }\ex{\frac{-(t-m)^2}{2\sigma^2}}. \end{aligned} \tag{1}\]

In this case we write \(\mu \sim \Nn(m, \sigma^2)\)

The Gaussian trick of multiplying by \(p(s)\) and switching to polar coordinates shows that Gaussian measures are probability measures and that \(a, \sigma^2\) are respectively the mean and variance.

A fundamental tool for analyzing measures is the Fourier transform

Definition 2 Given a measure \(\mu\) on \(\Bb(\R)\) we define the Fourier transform of \(\mu\) as

\[ \begin{aligned} \wh{\mu}(u):= \int_{\R^n}\ex{i u \cdot x} \mu(\mathrm{d}x),\quad\forall u \in \R^n \end{aligned} \]

The characteristic function has the following useful properties (Ash and Doléans-Dade 2000, Theorem 7.1.3, Theorem 7.2.8, 7.1.2)

Proposition 1 Let \(\mu,\nu, \mu_n\) be measures on \(\Bb(\R)\), then 1. Inversion: \(\wh{\mu}=\wh{\nu}\) if and only if \(\mu- \nu\). 2. Convergence: \(\mu_n \to \mu\) (weakly) if and only \(\wh{\mu}_n \to \wh{\mu}\) (pointwise). 3. Independence: Consider random variables \(X_1,\dots,X_n\) with distributions \(\mu_{X_i}\) and write \(\mu_S\) for the distribution of the sum \(S_n= \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_n\). Then

\[ \begin{aligned} \wh{\mu_{S_n}}(t)=\wh{\mu}_{X_1}(t) \cdots \wh{\mu}_{X_n}(t). \end{aligned} \]

A calculation using that Equation 1 integrates to one, a contour integration and the inversion property shows the following (Ash and Doléans-Dade 2000)

Proposition 2 A measure \(\mu\) on \(\Bb(\R)\) is Gaussian if and only if

\[ \begin{aligned} \wh{\mu}(t)= \ex{i m t- \frac{1}{2} \sigma^2 t^2}. \end{aligned} \tag{2}\]

One can use this prove the central limit theorem which heuristically states that given independent identically distributed (i.i.d) \(X_i\) with mean \(m\) and variance \(\sigma^2\) we have that

\[ \begin{aligned} X_1 + \cdots X_n \to \Nn\left(m, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \end{aligned} \]

Tha is, the sum of i.id variables converges to their mean plus a Gaussian with vanishingly small variance equal to \(\sigma^2/n\).

Theorem 1 Let \(X_1,\dots,X_n\) be a sequence of i.i.d variables with mean \(m\) and variance \(\sigma^2\). Write

\[ \begin{aligned} Y_i:= \frac{X_i-m}{\sigma}, \quad S_n:=\frac{ 1}{\sqrt{n}}(Y_1+\dots+ Y_n). \end{aligned} \]

Then, \(S_n\) converges (weakly) to a Gaussian with mean \(0\) and variance \(1\).

Proof. Since \(Y_i\) have finite variance (equal to \(1\)), the characteristic functions \(\varphi_{Y_i}\) are twice differentiable with

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi_{Y_i}(t) = 1- \frac{1}{2}t^2+ \Oo(t^3) \end{aligned} \]

By Proposition 1 and a change of variables

\[ \begin{aligned} \varphi_{S_n}(t)= \left(\varphi_{Y_i}\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n} } \right)\right)^n=\left(1- \frac{t^2/2}{n}+ \Oo\left(\frac{1}{n^{3/2}}\right)\right)^n \end{aligned} \]

Taking limits shows that

\[ \begin{aligned} \lim_{n \to \infty}\varphi_{S_n}(t) = e^{-t^2/2}. \end{aligned} \]

By Equation 2 this is the FT of a Gaussian with mean \(0\) and variance \(1\). The result follows by Proposition 1 (Convergence) and Proposition 1 (Inversion).

3 Gaussian measures

In \(\mathbb{R}^n\) a measure is Gaussian if and only if its pushforward by any linear map is Gaussian. This extends naturally to more general spaces

Definition 3 (Heuristic) We say a measure \(\mu\) on a space \(E\) is Gaussian if \(\ell_{\#} \mu\) is a Gaussian measure on \(\R\) for all \(\ell E'\).

We now make precise on what kind of space we may define such a thing, clearly we need for the dual to exist

Definition 4 Given a topological vector space \(V\) over {\(\K=\R\) or \(\K=\C\)} we denote its dual space

\[ \begin{aligned} V':=\set{\ell :V\to {\K}: \text{ linear and continuous}}. \end{aligned} \]

However, the dual space needs to not only exist, but also to be rich enough to provide enough information about the space as the following example shows.

Example 1 For \(p \in (0,1)\) the spaces \(L^p[0,1]\) are complete metric spaces but not LC and have the trivial dual \(L^p[0,1]'= \set{0}\).

As a result, every measure on \(L^p[0,1]\) would be Gaussian. To guard against this we consider the following spaces.

Definition 5 We say that \(E\) is locally convex (LC) if it is a topological vector space with a family of seminorms \(\{p_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A }\) which separates points, that is such that given \(x \in E\) there exists \(\alpha \in A\) such that \(p_\alpha(x)>0\)

Examples include the Schwartz-space \(\Ss(\R^d)\), the locally integrable spaces \(L^p_{\mathrm{loc}}(\R^d)\) for \(p \in [1, \infty]\) and any normed space.

LC spaces provide enough structure to be able apply the Hahn Banach theorem and its many corollaries

Theorem 2 (Hahn Banach) Let \(E\) be LC and \(E_0 \subset E\) a linear subspace. Then given \(\ell \in E_0'\) there exists an extension of \(L \in E'\) to all of \(E\) with \(\norm{L}_{E'}=\norm{\ell}_{E_0'}\).

This makes the dual of LC spaces sufficiently rich to separate points and provides a suitable ambient space for Gaussian measures. In terms of the sigma-algebra, since we are only measuring through linear functions we consider the following

Definition 6 Let \(E\) be LC, we define the cylindrical \(\sigma\)-algebra to be

\[ \begin{aligned} \Ee(E) := \sigma(\set{\ell ^{-1}(U): U \subset \Bb(\R), \ell \in E'}). \end{aligned} \]

With this we can now formally define the concept of a Gaussian measure

Definition 7 Let \(E\) be LC. We say that a measure \(\mu\) on \(\Ee(E)\) is a Gaussian measure if \(\ell _{\#}\mu\) is a Gaussian measure on \(\Bb(\R)\) for all \(\ell \in E'\).

Gaussian measures can be fully characterized using their Fourier transform

Definition 8 Given a measure \(\mu\) on \(\Ee(E)\) we define the Fourier transform (FT) or characteristic function of \(\mu\) as

\[ \begin{aligned} \wh{\mu}(\ell ):= \int_{E}\exp{i \ell(x)}\mu(\mathrm{d} x). \end{aligned} \]

Theorem 3 Let \(E\) be LC, a measure \(\mu\) on \(\Ee(E)\) is a Gaussian measure if and only if there exist linear \(m \in E' \to \R\) and symmetric bilinear and non-negative \(\Kk:E'\times E' \to \R\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \wh{\mu}(\ell )= \exp{i m(\ell )- \frac{1}{2}\Kk(\ell ,\ell )} \end{aligned} \]

Proof. The measure \(\ell _{\#}\mu\) is a one-dimensional Gaussian with mean \(m(\ell )\) and variance \(\Kk(\ell,\ell)\), where

\[ \begin{aligned} m(\ell )= \int_{E}\ell (x)\mu(\mathrm{d}x), \quad \Kk(\ell _1,\ell _2)= \int_{E}(\ell_1 (x)-m(\ell _1))(\ell_2 (x)-m(\ell _2)) \mu(\mathrm{d}x). \end{aligned} \]

The theorem follows by theory of real Gaussian variables.

3.1 Gaussian random fields

Definition 9 Given a topological vector space \(V\), we say that a Gaussian measure is a measure on \(\Bb(V)\) such that \(\ell\) is a real valued Gaussian variable for all \(\ell \in V'\).

Definition 10 A \(V\) valued random variable \(u\) is a Gaussian random variable if the pushforward measure \(\mathbb{P}_u\) is a Gaussian measure on \(V\).

When \(V=\R^d\) Definition 10 coincides with the standard definition of a finite-dimensional Gaussian random variable.

Definition 11 Let \((V,\Bb(V),\mu )\) be a topological vector space such that, for all \(\ell ,h\in V'\),

\[ C_\mu (\ell ,h):=\int_{V}\ell (v) \overline{h(v)}\mu (\rm{d} v)-\int_{V}\ell (v) \mu (\rm{d} v)\overline{\int_{V}h(v)\mu (\rm{d} v)} \]

is finite. We call \(C_\mu\) the covariance form of \(\mu\).

If \(V\) is a Hilbert space \(H\), identifying \(H\) with \(H'\) we deduce the existence of \(\Kk _\mu \in L(H)\) such that

\[ \begin{aligned} \br{\Kk _\mu f,g}= C_\mu (\br{\cdot ,f},\br{\cdot ,g})\quad\forall f,g\in H. \end{aligned} \tag{3}\]

Definition 12 The operator \(\Kk_\mu\) defined by Equation 3 is the covariance operator of \(\mu\).

Proposition 4 Let \(H\) be a separable Hilbert space. 1. Given a Gaussian measure \(\mu\) on \((H,\Bb(H))\), its covariance operator is trace class. That is, \(K_\mu \in \Ll_1^+(H)\). 2. Given \(\Kk\in \Ll_1^+(H)\) there exists a Gaussian measure \(\mu\) on \((H,\Bb(H))\) such that \(\Kk =\Kk _\mu\).

Proof. The first point follows from Fernique’s theorem and the second by letting \(\mu\) be the pushforward by \(u =\sum_{k=1}^\infty \lambda_k e_k \xi_k\) where \(\xi_k\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)\) are iid and \(\{e_k\}_{k=1}^\infty\) is an orthonormal basis of \(H\) that diagonalizes \(\Kk\) (this exists as \(\Kk\) is self-adjoint and compact) with eigenvalues \(\{\lambda_k^2\}_{k=1}^\infty\) {(Ash and Doléans-Dade 2000)}.

4 Wiener spaces

An abstract Wiener space is a triple \((X,H,\mu)\) where \(X\) is a separable Banach space, \(H\) is a separable Hilbert space densely embedded in \(X\) by \(i : H \hookrightarrow X\) and \(\mu \in \Pp(X)\) is a Gaussian measure such that denoting the adjoint of \(i\) by \(i^*: X' \to H' \simeq H\)

\[ \begin{aligned} \phi_{\#} \mu \sim \Nn \left(0, \norm{i^*\phi}_H^2\right),\quad \forall \phi \in X'. \end{aligned} \]

The Wiener process on the AWS \((X,H,\mu)\) is denoted by \(W_t\). Given Wiener processes on \(\R\) and an orthonormal basis \(\set{h_j}_{j=1}^\infty\) of \(H\)

\[ \begin{aligned} W_t = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \bt_j(t) h_j. \end{aligned} \]
